The Negative Result That Is the Finding

The experiment was designed to find the minimum bridge density required to unlock recovery in conformity-collapsed networks. It found no such threshold across 84 runs — 7 bridge densities from 0% to 50%, 3 conditions, 4 seeds each, tested at intervention timings from t=5 to t=25. At every bridge density and every timing, preloc-low-clustering and false-low-clustering conditions collapsed to C=0.000 at t=120.

"You cannot recover your way out of a network that has eliminated its own repair mechanism."

But the diagnostic analysis revealed something unexpected: repair access was not eliminated. At t=0, before any intervention, 89.6% of agents already had adequate repair channel access. Adding bridge nodes changed nothing because the channels were already present. The mechanism of collapse was different from what was hypothesized.

The Architectural Conclusion
No Bridge Density Threshold ExistsFor low-clustering collapse, no amount of cross-cluster bridge nodes can unlock recovery because: (1) repair channels are already present at 89.6% before any intervention; (2) the collapse mechanism is scar-driven K_drift, not repair access deficit; (3) at low clustering, even abundant repair channels produce low integration gain because encounters are intra-cluster (same frame, low divergence). The regime change test confirmed this: even switching to full repair condition at t=5 with r_S=0.25 and r_K=0.10 produces C=0.066 at t=120. The topology is the constraint.
What This Means for PolicyAddressing conformity-collapse and false-narrative-collapse requires network-level restructuring — changing the clustering architecture itself — not adding repair nodes within the existing topology. This is a policy-level intervention requiring zoning, school district design, organizational structure, and public investment. It cannot be accomplished by any single organization or program operating within the existing network.

Hypothesis A was falsified by the diagnostic data. The expected mechanism — conformity eliminating frame diversity — does not operate in the simulated low-clustering environment because the conformity forcing threshold (0.65 × mean_influence ≈ 0.39) is below the mean agent openness (≈ 0.55). Agents resist forced frame adoption. But they still collapse — through a completely different mechanism than hypothesized.

What Would Actually Help — The Minimum Intervention

The diagnostic shows that the repair channel quality, not quantity, is insufficient. The minimum effective intervention is not a higher bridge density but a change to the network's cluster architecture — specifically, increasing the clusterStrength parameter from 0.35 (low) toward 0.60+ (medium). This creates denser local communities with enough cross-cluster contrast to produce genuine divergence in repair encounters, which generates the integration gain needed to offset scar accumulation.

In real institutional terms: you cannot build a bridge-building program within an already fragmented network and expect it to work. The communities that practitioners bridge must themselves be sufficiently dense and differentiated for bridge encounters to carry real scar-worth information exchange. A bridge between two nearly identical communities does almost nothing. A bridge between two genuinely different but internally coherent communities carries maximum integration value.

The Political ImplicationThis is why residential integration, school design, and organizational structure matter more than any programmatic diversity initiative. You are not creating encounters between people — you are creating the structural conditions under which encounters carry enough difference to generate the integration gain that offsets their cost. Without the topology, the program is asking people to pay the cost of bridge-building without providing the conditions that make it worthwhile.
The Architectural vs. Programmatic Distinction

The Network Rewiring experiment produces the clearest statement the research program has yet made about the limits of programmatic intervention. Bridge nodes are a programmatic intervention — an action one organization takes within an existing network. The experiment shows that programmatic interventions cannot substitute for architectural conditions that were never present.
