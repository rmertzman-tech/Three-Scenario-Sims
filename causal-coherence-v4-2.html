The QBox Bridge

Why causal order is the right starting point

QBox is a framework for quantum computation in which the causal order of operations is not fixed but can itself be in a superposition state. It provides a physical substrate in which causal structure is not globally primitive but emerges through local measurement. This is the conceptual stress-test environment: if causation can be genuinely indeterminate at the physical level, what happens to agents who must construct causal narratives as a precondition for coherent action?

Three claims ground this simulation’s relationship to QBox:

QBox: Causal structure can be genuinely underdetermined. Two events may not have a globally fixed ordering. This is not epistemic uncertainty about a fixed fact — it is ontological indeterminacy in the causal structure itself.
Agents: To act coherently, agents must construct a determinate causal ordering from indeterminate substrate. This is not a failure or a shortcut — it is the fundamental operation of any information-processing system embedded in a causally complex world.
UCP: Models the cost of that construction. When two agents stabilize different orderings from the same indeterminate substrate, neither is wrong. But the divergence between their constructions carries real costs — for coordination, for scar accumulation, and for the integrity of the interpretive architectures doing the construction.

The simulation does not claim to implement QBox physics. It implements the agent-level consequences of causal indeterminacy — treating the quantum substrate as a source of genuine ambiguity that forces agents into frame-construction, then modeling what happens to those frames under five different social conditions.

New in Version 3: What Network Topology Changes

Clustering and the acceleration of false consensus

Version 2 showed that premature consensus produces K_drift in a two-agent system. Version 3 shows that network clustering dramatically accelerates this dynamic at scale. When agents are organized into tightly coupled clusters, conformity pressure compounds — high-influence agents within a cluster drive their neighbors toward frame adoption, and those neighbors in turn apply pressure to their neighbors. The result is that K_drift spreads through a cluster faster than the network-level divergence metrics would suggest. The cluster looks coherent from outside while experiencing rapid internal kernel erosion.

The polarization metric captures a dynamic Version 2 could not model: a network can simultaneously show high within-cluster coherence and high between-cluster divergence. This is the formal structure of political polarization. Each community is internally aligned; the communities have become incompatible as interpretive systems. The risk is not simply that they disagree — it is that their kernels have drifted far enough from each other that repair has become structurally inaccessible without first restoring the repair capacity that chronic divergence has exhausted.

The repair access parameter

In real networks, repair-capable connections are not uniformly distributed. Some relationships have the trust, time, and protocol for genuine reconciliation. Most do not. The repair access slider controls the fraction of network edges that are repair-capable. At low repair access, even a genuine repair condition produces K_drift accumulation because most agents cannot reach the reconciliation process. This is the institutional design implication: the structural availability of repair protocols matters as much as the will to use them.

“A community can be internally coherent and collectively fragmented simultaneously. The within-cluster coherence is not the same as the network-level coherence. The surface metrics of agreement mask the deeper metric of whether repair remains structurally accessible.”
