What the Economy Does to Coherent Agency

Economic Systems and the Ethics of Institutional Architecture

§1 — The Suppression Gap at Economic Scale

The foundational arc built a framework. You practiced that framework on cases drawn from individual lives, ecological movements, political transitions, scientific methodology, and economic modeling. Now the applied sequence asks a harder question: what does the Agency Conditions Principle require of institutions — not of persons under institutional pressure, but of the institutions themselves?

Chapter 7 showed you what Doyne Farmer found: the representative agent assumption makes certain populations structurally invisible, and that invisibility is not a technical artifact — it is a policy consequence. This chapter asks what that finding means for institutional design.

The Central ACP Question for Economic Systems

Which populations' agency conditions does this economic architecture preserve? Which does it export? And what is it handing forward to the next generation?

This is not a question about whether the economy is growing. It is a question about whether growth is being produced by drawing on human capacity or by drawing it down — whether the economy is functioning more like the Green Belt Movement (Chapter 4) or more like the Rust Belt (Chapter 9).

One structural context before proceeding. Economic systems sit within a civilizational stack that runs from planetary biophysical limits at the top — the hard thermodynamic ceiling within which all economic activity must ultimately operate — down through international economic governance (trade agreements, capital mobility rules, currency regimes), to national policy, to markets and firms, to individual agents.

This chapter examines the institutional level. What looks like a stable coherence-preserving configuration here may be structurally unstable without corresponding governance at the levels above it. The applied sequence's later chapters will examine what coherence-preserving governance requires at civilizational scale.

Two Questions the Simulation Cannot Settle

The simulation models structural tendencies, not specific policy outcomes. It cannot tell you whether a particular minimum wage number produces employment gains or losses — that is a question for economists and the empirical literature.

What it can do is model whether an economic architecture preserves or exports the conditions under which coherent agency is possible. A policy can produce acceptable aggregate outcomes while simultaneously destroying the agency conditions of the populations it affects. That second kind of harm — harm to conditions rather than outcomes — is what you are learning to see in this chapter.

§2 — What This Chapter Is About

The simulation in this chapter models three economic architectures. Each one represents a structural configuration — a particular combination of evidence weighting, block fields, and environmental conditions — that either preserves or erodes the agency conditions of the populations inhabiting it.

Before moving to the nine fairness dimensions, make sure you can answer this question: in the Challenger case, what was the difference between λ Raw and λ Perceived, and what caused it? The economic architecture simulation uses the same structural logic at a different scale.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter you should be able to: identify the structural difference between entropy-exporting and coherence-preserving economic architectures; apply the nine fairness dimensions as coherence conditions; run the three simulation configurations and interpret what each suppression gap value means; apply the temporal commons concept to economic design; and use SAGE to develop an ethically grounded response to a specific economic policy scenario.

The Nine Fairness Dimensions as Coherence Conditions

Eric Beinhocker's framework for fair social contracts proposes nine dimensions of fairness that large-scale human collaboration depends on. These were derived independently from research on what makes social contracts functionally stable. The alignment with the EBB framework is evidence, not coincidence — each dimension is a different way the suppression gap can manifest at economic-institutional scale.

Coherence Preconditions

Agency
Maps to λ Raw. Whether agents have genuine capacity to make meaningful choices, or are structurally constrained to react. Chronic high-entropy survival mode — the Sapolsky gate substantially closed — erodes this dimension at the biological level.
Inclusion
Maps to community block field. Whether the architecture actually reaches the populations it claims to serve. The representative agent assumption is an inclusion failure at the modeling level.
Dignity
Maps to evidence weighting. A system that weights certain populations' testimony at near-zero produces a suppression gap regardless of what those populations actually know or need.

Institutional-Temporal Stabilizers

Rules-Based Order
Maps to entropy conditions. Unpredictable, arbitrarily enforced rules create high environmental entropy. High entropy degrades prospective coherence — the ATCF component that enables planning and forward projection.
Meritocratic Legitimacy
Maps to evidence architecture. Does the institution weight contributions in proportion to their actual quality, or route some contributions around decision processes regardless of quality? The Thiokol teleconference failed this dimension.
Security
Maps to stress and the Sapolsky gate. Chronic economic insecurity — housing instability, medical debt, wage volatility — suppresses deliberate agency at the biological level regardless of what policies nominally offer.

Forward-Horizon Conditions

Capabilities
Maps to prospective coherence / ATCF. Not what resources people have but what they can actually do and become. Negative community blocks in the presence of high entropy ensure λ Perceived stays low even when λ Raw holds.
Reciprocity
Maps to entropy-exporting mechanism. A system that offloads its disorder onto workers and communities while reporting stable aggregate metrics runs an accounting system that makes the extraction invisible.
Progress
Maps to temporal commons. Is the arc genuinely forward-moving for the populations inhabiting the system? This is the inter-generational question: what agency conditions is this architecture handing to the next generation?

§4 — The Temporal Commons

Every chapter in the foundational arc asked about the present: what are the agency conditions right now for the agents inhabiting this structure? The ACP's temporal commons concept asks a different question: what is this architecture handing forward?

Three Temporal Commons Questions

The Metabolic Question

Is this economic architecture sustaining human capacity or consuming it? An economy that extracts labor intensity, concentrates gains, and exports the costs onto workers' health, families, and communities is consuming human capacity faster than it is restoring it. The distinction between a healthy metabolism and a depleting one is not visible in GDP statistics. It is visible in FHRI measures, in burnout rates, in population health gradients, and in what the simulation models as morphic suppression accumulating across generations.

The Generative Question

What kind of agency capacity is the economic architecture handing to the next generation? A stable economy that provides adequate security floors, predictable rules, and genuine inclusion is handing forward structural conditions that enable children to develop the historical continuity, present integration, prospective coherence, and meta-constructor capacity that the ATCF measures. An economy that concentrates economic power while creating permanent precarity for a significant minority is handing forward a bifurcated agency landscape regardless of aggregate performance metrics.

The Civic Question

Does the economic architecture sustain the deliberative capacity that democratic governance requires? Chapter 9 showed you that World Values Map positions are thermodynamic facts — where populations land on the security/self-expression axis is substantially determined by the material conditions they inhabit. An economy that keeps a significant population in permanent survival mode is not just producing economic harm; it is producing the conditions under which democratic deliberation becomes structurally difficult.

A Fourth Question — The Civilizational Level

There is a temporal commons question that operates above the national level, and it is the hardest one the ACP raises. Climate change is the largest inter-generational cost externalization in human history: economic systems organized around short-run output have been exporting entropy onto the planetary systems that all future agency conditions depend on.

No national wage floor, labor protection, or redistributive policy can address this by itself. The suppression gap here operates between what the scientific community knows about the trajectory of planetary conditions and what reaches the international governance architectures that would need to act on it.

The entropy-exporting institution the ACP is ultimately most concerned about is civilization itself, when organized around architectures that cannot account for what they are handing forward. The applied sequence's existential risk chapter will address this directly. But students who have understood the temporal commons concept at the institutional level need to see it applied here, at the level where the stakes are highest and the governance architecture is most underdeveloped.

The Temporal Commons Test

Apply this test to any economic architecture or policy proposal:

Will the people who inhabit this structure for twenty years enter the next twenty years with more or less capacity for coherent agency than they entered the first twenty with?

If the honest answer is less — if the architecture systematically consumes human coherence capacity faster than it restores it — then the architecture is entropy-exporting regardless of its output metrics.

§5 — SAGE Walkthrough: The Minimum Wage Case

A mid-sized city is debating whether to raise the minimum wage from $10 to $15 per hour. The business association argues the increase will cause job losses. The labor coalition argues the current wage keeps full-time workers in chronic economic insecurity that prevents genuine economic agency. Both arguments are empirically contested. What does SAGE add?

See

The standard debate sees two aggregate effects: employment and wages. SAGE asks you to see more specifically. What does chronic wage insecurity do to prospective coherence — the capacity to plan, to invest in longer-term choices? What is the suppression gap between what these workers actually know about their economic situation and what reaches the policymakers making decisions about it?

See also the businesses. A business that cannot sustain itself at $15 per hour is either operating in a market where margins are too thin, or it is depending on labor compensation below what the ACP's security dimension requires. Both are structural facts, not individual failures.

Analyze

Apply the traditions from Chapter 8 that are most precise for this case:

Consequentialism: what does the best available evidence say about aggregate effects at this scale in this market? Note that Chapter 7 showed you why aggregate models may miss distributional effects that matter most. A net-zero employment effect at the aggregate level can mask significant harm to specific populations.

Deontology: is there a duty not to maintain a wage structure that keeps full-time workers in conditions of chronic insecurity? The ACP's security dimension suggests there is. What does the dignity dimension require: that workers be treated as genuine economic agents whose contribution warrants a wage that supports coherent agency?

Capabilities approach: can workers at the current minimum wage exercise genuine economic capability — access education, maintain housing stability, care for dependents, participate in civic life — or does the current structure suppress those capabilities regardless of their nominal availability?

Generate

A tiered wage law (smaller businesses get a slower increase timeline) is a real policy option. SAGE asks you to evaluate it against the nine fairness dimensions before recommending it. Does a tiered timeline preserve the rules-based order dimension — or does it create a two-tier labor market where workers in small businesses have structurally lower security and dignity for reasons that have nothing to do with their contribution?

Generate at least two alternatives. One option to consider: what happens if the wage floor increase is coupled with mechanisms that reduce entropy conditions for small businesses — simplified compliance, reduced administrative costs, targeted investment? The coherence-preserving approach does not simply redistribute within an entropy-exporting architecture. It asks what structural changes would reduce entropy for everyone.

Engage, Evaluate, and Iterate

Economic policy operates in complex adaptive systems. A $15 minimum wage implemented in one market had different effects than the same policy in a different market, and both had different effects than simple models predicted. The commitment to engage — to act on the best available structural analysis even under uncertainty — paired with the commitment to evaluate and iterate is what distinguishes ACP-grounded policy from both paralysis and overconfidence.

The simulation gives you structural tendencies. The research literature gives you empirical evidence. Your PRF gives you the interpretive lens through which you see both. SAGE holds all three in productive tension.
