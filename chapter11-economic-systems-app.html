§1 — The Suppression Gap at Economic Scale

The foundational arc built a framework. You practiced that framework on cases drawn from individual lives, ecological movements, political transitions, scientific methodology, and economic modeling. Now the applied sequence asks a harder question: what does the Agency Conditions Principle require of institutions — not of persons under institutional pressure, but of the institutions themselves?

Chapter 7 showed you what Doyne Farmer found: the representative agent assumption makes certain populations structurally invisible, and that invisibility is not a technical artifact — it is a policy consequence. This chapter asks what that finding means for institutional design.

The Central ACP Question for Economic Systems

Which populations’ agency conditions does this economic architecture preserve? Which does it export? And what is it handing forward to the next generation?

This is not a question about whether the economy grows. It is a question about what kind of agency capacity the growth preserves, depletes, or destroys — and for whom. GDP measures output. FHRI_max measures the structural ceiling on human flourishing that the institutional architecture permits. These are different numbers, and they can move in opposite directions.

The three simulation configurations (Entropy-Exporting, Coherence-Preserving, BioBond Transition) model what happens to agents inside different economic architectures. The IPS parameters explain why those configurations produce the outcomes they do — which ownership structures, incentive systems, and regulatory burdens generate the suppression gap you observe in the simulation. §2 — Two Questions the Simulation Cannot Settle

What the Simulation Shows

The simulation shows the relationship between structural configuration and coordination capacity. It demonstrates that authority-weighted evidence, high entropy, and negative community block fields produce a suppression gap — that λ Perceived collapses relative to λ Raw when the institutional architecture routes certain signals around decisions.

What the simulation cannot settle: whether the GDP costs of Configuration B outweigh its coordination benefits, and whether Configuration B is structurally stable without international governance coordination that prevents race-to-the-bottom dynamics. These are contested empirical questions that the model cannot answer from within its own architecture.

Two Questions the Simulation Cannot Settle

The cost-benefit question: Does the coordination gain from Configuration B justify its GDP costs? This requires empirical data on the relationship between coordination capacity and long-run economic output across many institutional contexts — data that exists but is contested.

The stability question: Is Configuration B stable without international coordination? Mobile capital can exit coherence-preserving national architectures unless international governance prevents it. This is the legitimacy problem the course will address in Chapter 12.

The Nine Fairness Dimensions as Coherence Conditions

Eric Beinhocker’s framework for fair social contracts proposes nine dimensions of fairness that large-scale human collaboration depends on. These were derived independently from research on what makes social contracts functionally stable. The alignment with the EBB framework is evidence, not coincidence — each dimension is a different way the suppression gap can manifest at economic-institutional scale.

Coherence Preconditions

►
Agency
Maps to λ Raw. Whether agents have genuine capacity to make meaningful choices. When economic precarity constrains deliberation to immediate survival, λ Raw collapses regardless of formal freedom.
IPS parameter: μ (incentive misalignment) directly erodes agency by restructuring evaluation criteria around compliance rather than genuine choice.
Security
Maps to entropy export floor. Whether the architecture maintains the biological minimum for coherent agency — the Sapolsky floor below which glucocorticoid stress degrades prefrontal function.
IPS parameter: φ (physical entropy) is the direct measure of security failure — infrastructure degradation and staffing gaps that generate biological stress.
Rules
Maps to morphic suppression. Predictable, intelligible rules support planning. Opaque, frequently-changed rules generate learned self-censorship as agents stop investing in understanding systems that may change arbitrarily.
IPS parameter: σ (morphic residue) accumulates when rules are generated by crisis rather than evidence — each crisis adds a procedural layer that obscures rather than clarifies.
Voice
Maps to routing quality. Whether agent knowledge and preferences actually reach decisions. High routing quality requires that agent signal passes through institutional filters without systematic attenuation.
IPS parameter: ρ (regulatory burden) consumes the time and attention needed for meaningful voice participation.

Institutional-Temporal Stabilizers

►
Opportunities
Maps to FHRI_max. The structural ceiling on what coordination capacity an agent can achieve within this architecture, regardless of their individual effort or talent.
The PE nursing home case: FHRI_max = 13.6 means a structurally committed staff member can achieve at most 13.6 points of coordination capacity — not because they lack effort but because the architecture absorbs the remainder.
Reciprocity
Maps to OS2 Bridge. Whether agents’ coordination investments are matched by institutional response — whether contribution builds toward something or disappears into the suppression gap.
High OS2 Bridge requires low μ — incentive structures that reward coordination rather than compliance with metrics that extract without reciprocating.
Common Good
Maps to trust vector trajectory. Whether the economic architecture generates positive-sum coordination outcomes across populations, or zero-sum extraction that depletes collective coordination capacity.
High ε (extraction rate) is the primary driver of trust vector erosion across institutions — the mechanism by which private gain becomes public coordination loss.

Forward-Horizon Conditions

►
Proportionality
Maps to the RQ ceiling equation. Whether economic returns are proportional to productive contribution rather than to position in the extraction hierarchy.
The empirical calibration: wε=0.45 means extraction rate dominates the ceiling equation — disproportionate returns to extraction are the primary driver of coordination quality collapse.
Rights
Maps to the EE floor. The minimum conditions below which rights become formal guarantees with no substantive content — the biological floor without which autonomy and participation cannot be inhabited.
Non-profit facilities (EE floor = 0.195) can maintain rights in substance. PE-acquired facilities (EE floor = 0.553) cannot — the structural stress load makes substantive rights inaccessible.
Legitimacy
Maps to procedural vs. substantive legitimacy — the central distinction Chapter 12 will develop. Whether the institution’s authority rests on following correct procedures or on actually routing knowledge to decisions.
An institution can be procedurally legitimate while structurally delegitimate — following all the rules while the suppression gap absorbs the agent population’s capacity.

UCP Simulation — Three Economic Architectures

This simulation models three structural configurations. You are not testing whether a specific minimum wage produces employment gains or losses. You are modeling what happens to coordination capacity, suppression gap, and morphic suppression when agents inhabit three different economic architectures.

What Each Configuration Models

Configuration A — Entropy-Exporting: authority-weighted evidence (employer/capital signal dominant), negative fairness and community block fields, high entropy reflecting unprotected labor market volatility, high stress reflecting chronic economic precarity. Watch λ Perceived collapse relative to λ Raw and morphic suppression build across steps.

Configuration B — Coherence-Preserving: empirically-calibrated evidence weighting, positive block fields, lower entropy reflecting institutional predictability, lower stress reflecting adequate security floors. Watch λ Perceived approach λ Raw and OS2 Bridge become possible.

Configuration C — BioBond Transition: begins in Configuration A's conditions, institutional support activates at step 4 (modeling wage floor, labor protections, or community investment requirements). Watch the lag: morphic suppression accumulated in steps 1–3 does not immediately collapse when the BioBond activates.

IPS Economic Diagnostic — Structural Architecture and FHRI_max

Research foundation: Peterson et al. and the CMS public record. The healthcare simulations below draw on the published research of Dr. Lindsay Peterson (Associate Professor of Research, University of South Florida School of Aging Studies) and her colleagues at the Florida Policy Exchange Center on Aging. Their 2025 AARP Florida report — Change of Ownership and Quality in Florida Nursing Homes — documented how private equity acquisition of 156 Florida facilities between 2019 and 2024 produced a 13% decline in direct care staffing, a doubling of one-star quality ratings, and $1,000 higher Medicare costs per resident. The IPS parameter values displayed here are derived directly from these published findings and from publicly accessible federal databases: the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services Payroll-Based Journaling staffing dataset, the Provider Information file, the Ownership disclosure file, and the Health Deficiencies citation database.
Why this matters beyond Florida. Florida’s nursing home sector is a leading indicator, not an isolated case. Private equity acquisition of long-term care facilities is accelerating nationally. The structural dynamics documented in Florida — extraction-driven staffing reduction, billing-metric incentive restructuring, and quality rating decline — are visible in the federal data wherever ownership has transferred to investment vehicles that prioritize short-term surplus extraction over care coordination. This is SAGE applied to public data: Seeing what Peterson’s observational study documented, Analyzing the structural mechanism through IPS parameters, Generating an intervention framework, and Engaging with the ethical question of what ownership structures society should permit in settings that govern the final years of vulnerable lives.

Four Ownership Archetypes — Side-by-Side IPS Comparison

►

All four facility types operate under identical regulatory burden (ρ = 0.700) — the same CMS documentation and MDS compliance requirements apply regardless of ownership. The differences in FHRI_max are produced entirely by ownership-structure differences in ε, φ, σ, and μ. The 41.5-point structural gap between PE-Acquired and Non-Profit facilities is not a gap in staff quality or regulatory environment. It is a gap in structural architecture.

The falsifiable prediction: BioBond-equivalent interventions (HPRD improvement, stress reduction, training investment) at a PE-acquired Florida facility will produce approximately 44% of the coordination improvement they produce at a non-profit with equivalent baseline staff quality. Not because the interventions fail — but because the structural ceiling absorbs the remainder before it reaches care outcomes. This is testable against the public CMS data: if the model is correct, HPRD improvements at PE facilities should show approximately 44 cents of quality-metric improvement per dollar compared to non-profits making equivalent investments.

PE Acquisition Trajectory — 2019 to 2024

►

Year-by-year IPS evolution of a facility acquired by private equity in 2020. The pre-acquisition baseline reflects a moderate for-profit profile (FHRI_max = 35.4). The acquisition year shows the ε jump that occurs when ownership transfers to an investment-vehicle management structure. Subsequent years show φ accumulation (no capital reinvestment) and σ ratcheting (deficiency citations generate procedural layering). Note that ρ remains constant throughout — the CMS regulatory burden does not change with ownership. The Peterson et al. findings map directly: average star ratings fell from 3.4 (2019) to 2.9 (2024), and one-star facilities doubled from 10% to 21%.

What changed at acquisition, and what did not: The building was the same. The staff were the same people. The residents were the same population. The regulatory requirements were identical. What changed was a single financial transaction that restructured ε (surplus distribution away from reinvestment) and μ (staff evaluation criteria from care quality to billing metrics). FHRI_max collapsed from 35.4 to 19.2 in one year — a 46% decline in structural coordination capacity from a purely financial event.

BioBond — The Financial Instrument Response

►

The IPS analysis identifies the primary policy failure: agent-level wellness investment returns at approximately 44 cents on the dollar at PE-acquired facilities because the structural architecture absorbs the remainder. The obvious question is what financial instrument changes ε directly rather than working around it.

BioBond: Conditioning Returns on FHRI Improvement

A BioBond is a capital instrument whose investor returns are contingent on measured improvement in the FHRI composite score of the workforce it governs. The mechanism: investors who fund the transition from high-ε to lower-ε institutional architecture receive enhanced returns only when FHRI_max improves — verified through quarterly CMS PBJ staffing data and annual FHRI instrument administration.

Why this changes ε rather than working around it: Standard workforce wellness programs work within the existing ε structure, attempting to raise agent FHRI against a ceiling the structure imposes. BioBond instruments change the ceiling itself by making investor returns conditional on ε reduction. The investment thesis shifts from “extract surplus from the care labor margin” to “generate returns by demonstrating coordination improvement in a sector where coordination quality is measurably low and improving it has computable value.”

The Measurement Requirement

BioBond instruments require the measurement infrastructure that makes FHRI_max computable from observable data. The CMS public databases already provide proxies for ε (surplus distribution from financial filings), φ (staffing HPRD from PBJ), ρ (documentation-to-care ratio from MDS burden literature), σ (deficiency citation rate from Health Citations file), and μ (compliance-to-care evaluation ratio from HR documentation). The FHRI survey instrument adds the agent-level measurement that connects structural parameters to staff experience.

The Public Benefits Corporation role: The PBC that administers the FHRI pilot is not only a research organization. It is the measurement infrastructure that makes BioBond financial instruments viable — the entity that can certify FHRI_max improvement to institutional investors whose returns depend on it.

§4 — The Temporal Commons

Every chapter in the foundational arc asked about the present: what are the agency conditions right now for the agents inhabiting this structure? The ACP’s temporal commons concept asks a different question: what is this architecture handing forward?

Three Temporal Commons Questions

►

The Metabolic Question

Is this economic architecture sustaining human capacity or consuming it? An economy that extracts labor intensity, concentrates gains, and exports the costs onto workers’ health, families, and communities is consuming human capacity faster than it is restoring it. The distinction between a healthy metabolism and a depleting one is not visible in GDP statistics. It is visible in FHRI measures, in FHRI_max ceilings, in burnout rates, in population health gradients, and in what the simulation models as morphic suppression accumulating across generations.

The structural measure: An economic architecture’s metabolic health can now be given a precise number. The EE floor equation (0.40φ + 0.30σ + 0.25ε) is the rate at which the architecture exports entropy onto its agent population. An EE floor of 0.55 means 55% of the minimum entropy export is structurally imposed regardless of what agents do. This is the biological cost of the institutional architecture — the mandatory metabolic drain.

The Generative Question

What kind of agency capacity is the economic architecture producing in the next generation? A system that maintains coordination capacity in the current generation but exports φ (physical entropy through infrastructure underinvestment) is borrowing against the future. The children who grow up in high-φ environments — degraded schools, deferred maintenance, chronically understaffed care facilities — begin their adult agency at a lower FHRI_max than those who inherited well-maintained structural conditions.

This is the intergenerational dimension of the IPS analysis: φ accumulation is a transfer of physical entropy onto future populations who bear costs they did not incur and cannot refuse.

The Inheritance Question

What is this economic system handing forward? Not only material wealth, but structural conditions: the FHRI_max ceilings that the next generation will inhabit, the σ (morphic residue) accumulated from decades of crisis-driven procedural layering, the trust vector trajectories between institutions that are either building toward OS2 coordination or eroding toward mutual blocking.

The PE acquisition trajectory (2019→2024: FHRI_max from 35.4 to 15.2) is a temporal commons problem, not only an economic one. The 20-point structural decline is inherited by the next cohort of workers who enter those facilities, and by the next generation of residents who depend on them.

§5 — SAGE Walkthrough: The Minimum Wage Case

A mid-sized city is debating whether to raise the minimum wage from $10 to $15 per hour. The business association argues the increase will cause job losses. The labor coalition argues the current wage keeps full-time workers in chronic economic insecurity that prevents genuine economic agency. Both arguments are empirically contested. What does SAGE add?

See

The standard debate sees two aggregate effects: employment and wages. SAGE asks you to see more specifically. What does chronic wage insecurity do to prospective coherence — the capacity to plan, to invest in longer-term choices? What is the suppression gap between what these workers actually know about their economic situation and what reaches the policymakers making decisions about it?

See also the businesses. A business that cannot sustain itself at $15 per hour is either operating in a market where margins are too thin, or it is depending on labor compensation below what the ACP's security dimension requires. Both are structural facts, not individual failures.

Analyze

Apply the traditions from Chapter 8 that are most precise for this case:

Consequentialism: what does the best available evidence say about aggregate effects at this scale in this market? Note that Chapter 7 showed you why aggregate models may miss distributional effects that matter most. A net-zero employment effect at the aggregate level can mask significant harm to specific populations.

Deontology: is there a duty not to maintain a wage structure that keeps full-time workers in conditions of chronic insecurity? The ACP's security dimension suggests there is. What does the dignity dimension require: that workers be treated as genuine economic agents whose contribution warrants a wage that supports coherent agency?

Capabilities approach: can workers at the current minimum wage exercise genuine economic capability — access education, maintain housing stability, care for dependents, participate in civic life — or does the current structure suppress those capabilities regardless of their nominal availability?

Generate

A tiered wage law (smaller businesses get a slower increase timeline) is a real policy option. SAGE asks you to evaluate it against the nine fairness dimensions before recommending it. Does a tiered timeline preserve the rules-based order dimension — or does it create a two-tier labor market where workers in small businesses have structurally lower security and dignity for reasons that have nothing to do with their contribution?

Generate at least two alternatives. One option to consider: what happens if the wage floor increase is coupled with mechanisms that reduce entropy conditions for small businesses — simplified compliance, reduced administrative costs, targeted investment? The coherence-preserving approach does not simply redistribute within an entropy-exporting architecture. It asks what structural changes would reduce entropy for everyone.

Engage, Evaluate, and Iterate

Economic policy operates in complex adaptive systems. A $15 minimum wage implemented in one market had different effects than the same policy in a different market, and both had different effects than simple models predicted. The commitment to engage — to act on the best available structural analysis even under uncertainty — paired with the commitment to evaluate and iterate is what distinguishes ACP-grounded policy from both paralysis and overconfidence.

The simulation gives you structural tendencies. The research literature gives you empirical evidence. Your PRF gives you the interpretive lens through which you see both. SAGE holds all three in productive tension.
