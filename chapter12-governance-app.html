§I — The Knowledge-to-Decision Gap

▶

In 1988, James Hansen testified before the United States Senate that global warming had begun with a high degree of scientific confidence. The IPCC was established that same year. In the nearly four decades since, the gap between what climate science has established and what governance systems have done about it has remained one of the defining puzzles of modern political life. Every major scientific institution agrees on the basic facts. Multiple international agreements have been signed. Yet the gap between scientific consensus and policy action persists across jurisdictions, across governments, and across decades.

The standard explanation is political: climate action is blocked by powerful economic interests, by short-term electoral incentives, by ideological resistance, or by international coordination failures. These explanations are correct as far as they go. But they leave unexplained a prior structural question: why is it that in governance systems with formal procedural integrity — where elections happen, laws pass, courts review, and experts are consulted — the knowledge that those experts hold so consistently fails to reach the decisions that need it?

The Copernican Move for Governance. The individual-centered frame asks: why don't politicians do the right thing? The OS2 frame asks: what does the evidence architecture of this governance institution do to the routing of knowledge from expert and citizen populations into decisions? The first question generates moral evaluation of individuals. The second generates structural analysis of institutions. Chapter 12 pursues the second question — not because individual moral responsibility disappears, but because structural analysis is what identifies leverage points for repair.

This chapter uses the EBB simulation to make that structural question precise. The answer, as the simulation will show, is not primarily about individual failures of courage or integrity. It is about the evidence architecture of governance institutions — the distribution of epistemic weight across different categories of input that determines which knowledge is transmissible through the institutional filter from expert understanding toward decision authority.

§II — Procedural Legitimacy vs Routing Quality: Two Independent Variables

▶
Procedural Legitimacy

The degree to which a governance institution follows correct rules. Elections are held. Laws are debated. Courts follow precedent. Due process is observed. A governance institution can have high procedural legitimacy and low routing quality simultaneously — this is captured governance: the rules are followed, the forms are correct, but the evidence architecture is calibrated so that empirically-calibrated signal is systematically attenuated before reaching decision authority.

Routing Quality

The degree to which the knowledge and interests of the populations a governance institution serves actually reach its decisions. An institution with high routing quality has an evidence architecture calibrated to transmit empirical-calibrated signal from expert populations, value-calibrated signal from affected populations, and cross-worldview signal from diverse PRFs. Its decisions reflect what those populations know and care about.

The governance suppression gap — the central measurement of this chapter — is produced by high procedural legitimacy paired with low routing quality. This is precisely the configuration that Tim Palmer's climate projections face: high formal legitimacy as a knowledge source, low routing quality into governance decisions. The IPCC findings are not suppressed because anyone denies their procedural legitimacy. They are suppressed because the evidence architecture of climate governance is calibrated to weight authority-endorsed conclusions and short-term economic considerations far above empirically-calibrated long-horizon risk assessment.
Evidence Architecture: Three Compounding Filters
1
Evidence distribution policy
The population-level weighting of four epistemic processing modes: authority-weighted (defer to hierarchy), empirical-calibrated (update from data), tradition-weighted (follow precedent), alternative-facts (motivated reinterpretation). In Config A: 52% authority-weighted, 10% empirical-calibrated. In Config B: 52% empirical-calibrated, 18% authority-weighted. The IPCC's physical measurements are in the 10% pool in Config A.
2
Block fields — value-framing frictions
The structural amplifiers and penalties on how contributions are framed. A care-framed finding about vulnerable populations creates institutional friction in Config A (care = −0.25) regardless of its empirical quality — the evidence architecture penalizes that framing before the content is evaluated. In Config B, care-framed contributions advance deliberation (care = +0.35). The strongest signal in Config A is boundary (+0.40): protecting existing arrangements is the most structurally amplified input the institution receives.
3
Routing quality — the final filter
Distinct from evidence processing: the fraction of institutional deliberation output that actually reaches decision authority. Committee structures, agenda-setting gatekeepers, executive filtering. Config A routing quality = 0.22: even contributions that survive the first two filters are further attenuated before reaching the cabinet or regulatory body. Config B = 0.65. This filter operates on institutional output, not on individual contributions.
The compound effect: λ Perceived is the product of all three filters operating simultaneously. Config A's ~74% attenuation is not produced by any single parameter — it is produced by the evidence distribution, block fields, and routing quality all calibrated in the same direction against empirical-calibrated, long-horizon, care-framed knowledge. Each filter alone would reduce λ Perceived. All three together produce the governance suppression gap students will observe in the simulation.

§III — The Climate Governance Case Study

▶

This chapter uses climate governance as its primary case because it is the clearest contemporary example of the suppression gap operating at civilizational scale — and because it connects directly to the research programs the EBB simulation is designed to complement. Tim Palmer's ensemble forecasting at Oxford and Doyne Farmer's household-and-firm model at INET both assume their outputs will reach governance decisions. The governance suppression gap is the structural reason that assumption may not hold.

The Structure of the Gap

The expert knowledge of the scientific community is intact and substantial: decades of observational data, increasingly precise models, high-confidence projections. This is λ Raw for climate governance — the knowledge that exists and could in principle reach decisions. What reaches governance decisions — λ Perceived — is dramatically smaller. The evidence architecture of most national governance systems weights authority-weighted evidence (conclusions endorsed by economic ministries, energy incumbents, short-term budget authorities) most heavily, with empirical-calibrated evidence last.

Morphic Suppression in the Scientific Community

The simulation makes visible something that surveys of the scientific community confirm but that governance analysis rarely names: morphic suppression in the expert population. When empirically-calibrated contributions consistently fail to change governance outcomes — when decades of increasingly precise projections produce no proportionate policy response — the scientific community begins to learn that its signal does not reach outcomes.

This accumulated learned self-censorship is not always conscious. It manifests as the self-editing that occurs before writing a policy brief, the careful qualification of findings to avoid political controversy, the avoidance of the most alarming projections because previous experience has shown they produce backlash rather than action. The morphic suppression step chart in Configuration A — building steadily across each step — is the model's account of four decades of this dynamic. And it introduces a feedback loop: the suppression gap does not only prevent expert knowledge from reaching decisions. It gradually changes the quality of the contributions the system receives. When experts learn that precision produces no better outcomes than vagueness, they stop being precise.

§IV — The Four Traditions at Governance Scale

▶

Each ethical tradition has something to say about governance failure — and as in every previous chapter, each individual-level analysis is correct and insufficient. The OS2 column below makes the structural causal claim available. Read each row as: the left reading is not wrong; the right reading is what makes the left reading actionable.

Virtue Ethics Who are you becoming? ▾
OS1 — Individual Reading

Climate inaction reflects failures of practical wisdom and political courage. Officials who defer to incumbent interests rather than acting on expert knowledge are failing their virtue obligations. Better leaders with more courage would produce better outcomes.

OS2 — Structural Extension

What governance architecture produces or destroys the character of the officials who inhabit it? A governance institution whose reward structure systematically punishes engagement with empirical-calibrated long-horizon thinking and rewards short-term economic constituency responsiveness will produce officials whose practical wisdom is calibrated precisely against what the climate crisis requires — regardless of their individual character entering office.

Deontology (Kant) What is your duty? ▾
OS1 — Individual Reading

Governance officials have a categorical duty to act on the best available evidence about threats to the persons they govern. Failure to act on climate science is a Kantian violation — treating the bearers of future harm as means to short-term political survival rather than as ends in themselves.

OS2 — Structural Extension

What governance architecture enables rational agency — of both officials and citizens — to reach decisions? The suppression gap in climate governance is a structural Kantian violation: the institutional architecture consults experts to satisfy procedural requirements while routing their signal away from decision authority. It treats the bearers of empirical knowledge as means to legitimacy performance rather than sources of genuine input.

Consequentialism (Mill/Bentham) What does this produce? ▾
OS1 — Individual Reading

Climate governance failure produces immense aggregate harm. Officials have miscalculated expected value — the cost of action is less than the cost of inaction by any reasonable discount rate. Better information and better calculation would produce better outcomes.

OS2 — Structural Extension

What does this governance architecture produce as a population-level emergent pattern? The answer is not miscalculation but structural attenuation: the evidence architecture is calibrated to systematically underweight exactly the category of input (empirically-calibrated long-horizon risk) that correct expected value calculation would require to weight most heavily. The problem is not the calculation. It is the information that reaches the calculation.

Care Ethics (Noddings/Gilligan) Who are you in relation with? ▾
OS1 — Individual Reading

Climate governance fails to attend to the specific populations most vulnerable to climate harm — future generations, low-lying coastal communities, subsistence agricultural populations. Officials have failed their relational obligations to those they govern.

OS2 — Structural Extension

What governance architecture severs the relational networks through which affected populations' knowledge reaches decisions? The care block field in captured governance is negative: raising the human consequences of climate inaction creates friction rather than advancing deliberation. The relational infrastructure through which the most affected populations should be able to communicate their situation has been structurally severed — not by any individual official's indifference, but by an evidence architecture that treats care-framed contributions as off-topic.

Read this table as you have read all previous tradition tables: the OS1 column is correct and insufficient. Changing individual officials without changing the governance architecture will reproduce the same suppression gap from whoever replaces them. The architecture is the primary causal agent — which is also why it is the primary site of repair.

§V — Rawls, Nussbaum, and Governance Design

▶

The supplementary chapter on Modern Justice Theories provides the normative framework for what the simulation is measuring. Two principles apply directly to governance architecture.

Rawls — The Difference Principle Applied to Routing

Governance architecture is just only if it makes the routing quality for the least advantaged populations better, not only for the majority or the powerful. Config A routes the contributions of the most powerful constituencies most effectively. Config B gives all populations a more proportionate chance of reaching decisions. The Rawlsian judgment is clear regardless of which configuration produces better aggregate outcomes.

Nussbaum — The Capability Threshold for Political Participation

A governance institution whose routing quality is below the threshold required for genuine political participation is failing to provide the political control capability Nussbaum identifies as one of the ten central human capabilities. The morphic suppression trajectory in Config A is the simulation's account of what happens when routing quality is chronically below threshold: the capability is not simply unexercised — it is gradually degraded as accumulated experience changes what people believe is possible.

§VI — Reading the Simulation: What to Watch

▶
Config A — Captured Governance. Watch λ Perceived: it will be far below λ Raw (fixed at 0.72). That gap is the governance suppression gap — roughly 74% of available expert knowledge filtered before reaching decisions. Then watch the step chart: morphic suppression builds steadily from step 1 to step 8. Each step is another cycle of expert contribution entering the institutional filter, being attenuated, producing no outcome change. The trajectory is four decades of IPCC reports that changed the scientific certainty without changing the policy response.

Key question: the 21% that reaches decisions is not a random 21%. What specific categories of input survived the authority-weighted, boundary-protective filter?
Config B — Coherence-Preserving. Notice that λ Raw is lower than in Config A — around 0.29 rather than 0.72. This counterintuitive result is the chapter's most important conceptual transfer: Config B is not modeling a large body of expert knowledge being blocked. It is modeling genuine participatory deliberation where diverse forms of knowledge contribute collaboratively. The suppression gap is near zero. Morphic suppression is flat. OS2 Bridge is higher.

Key question: why is the ratio of λ Perceived to λ Raw closer to 1.0 in Config B, even though absolute λ Raw is lower? What does this reveal about the difference between capacity blocked and capacity expressed?

But morphic suppression does not fall at step 4. It continues building, more slowly, for steps 5, 6, 7 before finally decelerating. §VII — The Horizon of This Instrument

▶

The simulation models one governance institution. The climate governance challenge involves multiple institutional actors simultaneously: scientific bodies, regulatory agencies, legislative bodies, international treaty organizations, civil society groups, and incumbent economic interests. Each has its own InstitutionState. Each is connected to every other through trust vectors that have been building or decaying for decades.

The research extension uses Social Field Theory's potential energy formulation PE = −k(SiIj/rij), where 1/r is the trust vector Γ between institutional actors. When you run the current simulation, you are measuring the suppression gap within one institution. The research extension measures the suppression gap across institutional boundaries — whether what the scientific institution knows can reach the legislative institution's decisions across a trust vector that has been eroding since the first IPCC report. That measurement is what determines whether Tim Palmer's findings can reach the governance decisions that need them. It is the missing instrument identified in the "From Agent to Civilization" framework.

This limitation is not a failure of the current instrument. It is its precise horizon — the point where the current resolution ends and the next instrument begins. Every chapter of this course has taught you to read one level of the same measurement. Chapter 12 shows you the governance institution’s suppression gap. The research sequence that follows shows you the inter-institutional field that produces all of them.

Type 1 vs. Type 2 routing quality failure. The four simulation configurations reveal two structurally distinct failure modes that produce similar aggregate metrics but require completely different remedies.

Type 1 — Agent-level failure: Routing quality is low because the agent population has poor PRF alignment, low coherence, or inadequate evidence quality. The structural ceiling (FHRI_max) is high enough to permit better outcomes, but the current agent population is not reaching it. Remedy: agent-level intervention. Configuration B shows what this population can produce when structural conditions are right.

Type 2 — Structural ceiling failure (IPS): Routing quality is low because ε, μ, and ρ have imposed a ceiling below which no agent-level improvement can push outcomes. Configuration D makes this visible directly: agent FHRI = 82.0 (highly competent professionals), FHRI_max = 14.3. The 67.7-point gap is absorbed by the structural architecture before any of that competence reaches decisions. Remedy: structural reform. Agent-level intervention is wasted until the ceiling rises.

The diagnostic question: When you observe a governance institution with poor routing quality, which failure type is it? The simulation gives you the tool: run Config D parameters against a real institution’s known agent quality. If the suppression gap exceeds what agent quality explains, you are in a Type 2 failure — and the intervention strategy must change accordingly.

1. Evidence distribution — the population-level weighting of four distinct epistemic processing modes across the agent population:

Authority-weightedAgents defer to official hierarchy and credentialed institutions regardless of underlying evidence quality. In governance: deference to economic ministries, incumbent bodies, official position.
Empirical-calibratedAgents update beliefs based on observable data with minimal prior bias. Physical measurements, replicable findings, peer-reviewed analysis. The mode IPCC science operates in.
Tradition-weightedAgents prioritize precedent, continuity, established practice. What has worked before; what the institution has historically recognized as legitimate input.
Alternative-factsAgents actively reinterpret evidence to match prior beliefs. High interpretive override and motivated reasoning. In governance: noise generated by incumbent interest advocacy.

2. Block fields — structural frictions and facilitators on how contributions are framed. These are not evidence categories — they are the value and relational contexts in which evidence is presented:

Negative care/fairness/community means that contributions framed in terms of human consequences, distributional effects, or collective interests create institutional friction rather than advancing deliberation — regardless of their empirical quality. A care-framed IPCC finding about vulnerable populations is not just underweighted; it is actively penalized.

Positive boundary means contributions that protect existing arrangements are structurally amplified. In Config A, boundary = +0.40: the strongest single block field signal in the system favors the status quo.

3. Institution state → routing quality — distinct from evidence processing, this is the fraction of the institutional deliberation output that reaches actual decision authority. Policy committees, agenda-setting gatekeepers, executive filtering: even contributions that survive the evidence distribution and block field filters are further attenuated here. Config A routing quality = 0.22. Config B = 0.65.

λ Raw fixed (Config A & C): Expert knowledge and citizen understanding is held constant at 0.72. The architecture is the filter — not the capacity. Config B uses emergent λ Raw, reflecting what coherence-preserving deliberation produces naturally.

Evidence architecture (combined effect): λ Perceived is the product of all three filters operating simultaneously. The evidence distribution determines what epistemic processing modes dominate. The block fields determine which value-framed contributions advance or create friction. The routing quality determines how much of the institutional output reaches actual decision authority. Config A's ~74% attenuation is not produced by any one of these — it is produced by all three calibrated in the same direction against empirical-calibrated, long-horizon, care-framed knowledge. Config B's near-zero gap is produced by all three aligned in the other direction.

Reading Your Results — Common Prediction Errors

▶
Config A — Most common error: underestimating the suppression gap. Most students predict λ Perceived around 0.30–0.40, not 0.19. This error is instructive: if your PRF has high empirical-calibrated sensitivity, you implicitly expect governance institutions to weight empirical evidence similarly to how you weight it. The simulation is showing you that your expectation is wrong by a factor of three to four. The institutional evidence architecture you are measuring is calibrated by incentives and pressures that are structurally independent of your epistemic commitments.
Config B — Most common error: expecting λ Raw to be higher than Config A. Config B's λ Raw emerges around 0.29 — lower than Config A's fixed 0.72. Students often read this as Config B performing worse. The correct reading: Config B is not modeling a large blocked reservoir. It is modeling authentic participatory deliberation. The ratio of λ Perceived to λ Raw is near 1.0. Almost everything that is available reaches decisions. That is the achievement — not the absolute level, the ratio.
Config C — Most common error: expecting morphic suppression to fall immediately at step 4. Students who correctly predict that λ Perceived rises after the reform often simultaneously predict morphic suppression falling at the same moment. The step chart shows that suppression continues building (more slowly) for steps 5–7 before decelerating. The accumulated learned self-censorship of four decades does not reverse on contact with new institutional conditions. Structural repair changes the trajectory. It does not erase the history.
