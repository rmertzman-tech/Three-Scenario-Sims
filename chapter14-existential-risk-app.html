§I — The Leverage Hierarchy the Course Has Been Building

▶

Donella Meadows identified twelve places to intervene in a system, ordered from least to most effective. She published them in 1999 and expanded the account in Thinking in Systems (2008). The hierarchy is taught in systems thinking curricula worldwide as a general heuristic: changing parameters is low leverage, feedback loops higher, information flows higher still, rules, self-organization, goals, and paradigms upward from there.

What those curricula rarely do is connect the hierarchy to a measurement instrument. Meadows can tell you information flows are more powerful than parameters — but not by how much, in a specific system, for a specific intervention. The EBB simulation has been building that measurement capacity since Chapter 2. And Chapter 14 makes visible something that has been true throughout the course: every chapter has been teaching you to operate at a progressively higher level of the hierarchy.

§II — Two Failure Modes: The Iron Cage and the Obsolescence Trap

▶
The Iron Cage Attractor

The field configuration in which the high trust vector between national governance and the incumbent economic sector (Γ = 0.80 in the simulation) systematically overrides all L6 and L7 interventions. The incumbent sector's routing quality into governance is not accidental — it is actively maintained by structural investment in lobbying, regulatory access, and revolving-door mechanisms. The simulation shows this as a self-reinforcing pattern: the strong adverse trust vector ensures that alternative-facts entropy export reaches governance with high fidelity, degrading the evidence architecture that would otherwise route scientific findings.

The Obsolescence Trap

The failure mode when a scientific institution becomes so tightly bounded around physical precision that its output, even when the inter-institutional trust vector improves, cannot be received by the governance institution's deliberative capacity. High legitimacy, high internal coherence, high physical precision — but zero routing quality because the signal is not framed in vocabulary that governance institutions can process. Improving the trust vector alone is insufficient without simultaneously improving the translation capacity at the institutional boundary.

The AMC as a field configuration — not a normative proposal. The Adaptive Moral Commonwealth is the description of the inter-institutional field configuration that avoids both failure modes, specified in the model's vocabulary: (1) trust vector between scientific institution and national governance above coordination threshold, maintained by a structural mechanism with enforcement capacity; (2) treaty organization ↔ national governance above threshold, maintained by binding commitments rather than voluntary NDCs; (3) incumbent economic sector's routing quality into governance constrained by L6 rules preventing its entropy export from dominating the evidence architecture; (4) repair bandwidth within governance sufficient to incorporate evidence challenging existing trajectories — which requires L4 goal change. This is a specification of what the field needs to look like for coordinated response to be structurally available. Not a utopia. A measurement target.

Why L7 Is Insufficient Without L5

▶

Meadows identifies level 7 (information flows) as high leverage — far above parameters (L12) and feedback loop gain (L8-9). The simulation confirms this: the Ch12 Config A → Config B shift was a L7 intervention, and it produced a large improvement in routing quality within national governance. But the inter-institutional field simulation shows what single-institution analysis cannot: a L7 improvement within national governance does not propagate to the scientific institution unless the trust vector between them is above the coordination threshold.

The trust vector threshold is the L5 structural relationship that determines whether improvements at L6 and L7 can cross institutional boundaries. When the Scientific Body ↔ National Governance trust vector is at Γ = 0.22 (far below the 0.55 threshold), the routing success condition — λ_perceived × routing_quality × Γ > 0.50 — is never met. Scientific findings do not reach national governance deliberation regardless of what the evidence architecture within national governance does internally.

The specific intervention the model identifies. Restoring the Scientific Body ↔ National Governance trust vector from Γ = 0.22 to above Γ = 0.55 (a change of 0.33) would change the routing success condition from consistently failing to consistently meeting threshold. In the model's vocabulary, this requires: (1) a binding scientific advisory mechanism with enforcement capacity that increases routing_quality within national governance for scientific input specifically; (2) sustained demonstration over multiple steps that scientific contributions reach policy outcomes — which is the structural condition that allows the trust vector to build rather than erode. This is a L5 intervention in Meadows' terms: it changes the structural relationship, not only the parameters or rules within the existing structure.

The Adverse Trust Vector Problem

▶

The simulation reveals a structural feature that Meadows' hierarchy does not automatically surface: the existence of trust vectors that are above the coordination threshold but working against the coordination the field needs. The National Governance ↔ Incumbent Economic Sector trust vector (Γ = 0.80, the only pair above threshold) is not a coordination resource — it is a coordination obstacle. The incumbent sector's routing quality into national governance is 0.80; the scientific institution's effective routing quality (after the trust vector constraint) is approximately 0.22 × 0.35 = 0.08.

This means the adverse trust vector actively maintains the L7 blockage: the high-fidelity channel from the incumbent sector to national governance routes alternative-facts entropy export into the governance evidence architecture, degrading the routing conditions that scientific signal would need. Restoring the scientific ↔ governance trust vector is necessary but insufficient if the adverse trust vector simultaneously continues degrading the evidence architecture from the other side. The AMC field configuration requires both: trust vector restoration on the scientific side AND routing quality constraint on the incumbent side (L6 rules — evidence standards requirements, fiduciary disclosure mandates, regulatory capture prohibitions).

What the Simulation Demonstrates

▶

The climate governance field simulation is theoretically grounded and internally consistent. The underlying physics — the thermodynamic constraints on agent capacity, the Social Field Theory potential energy formula, the Meadows leverage hierarchy — are all derivable from first principles and validated across the course's case studies at smaller scales. The simulation produces findings that are consistent with what we know from the historical record of the IPCC-governance relationship. This gives us grounds to take the model's outputs seriously as a starting point for analysis.

The simulation puts the EBB framework in the same category as Palmer's first ensemble climate models in the early 1990s: theoretically grounded, physically principled, producing findings consistent with observed dynamics, but not yet validated against independent empirical data. Palmer's models became credible over decades as their predictions were tested against observations. The AMC simulation is at the beginning of that validation arc.

What the Simulation Projects, Not Proves

▶
Calibrated estimates, not measurements

The specific trust vector values (Γ = 0.42 for scientific ↔ governance, Γ = 0.80 for governance ↔ incumbent economic) are our best estimates derived from qualitative reading of the historical record — not independent measurements. Different calibration choices would produce different trajectories.

Theoretically motivated thresholds

The coordination threshold of 0.55 is theoretically motivated, not empirically derived from data about when inter-institutional coordination actually succeeds or fails. The ATCF validation study — running thousands of parameter sweeps — is what would make this threshold empirical rather than theoretical.

Calibrated dynamics, not measured rates

The trust build rate (0.010) and erode rate (0.025) are calibrated to produce dynamics that match the observed pattern, not measured from any dataset. The asymmetry (erodes faster than builds) is theoretically motivated by the historical record but not independently validated.

The epistemological standard being applied
A model is credible when (1) its theoretical foundations are sound, (2) its parameters are calibrated from the best available evidence, (3) its outputs are consistent with the historical record, and (4) it makes falsifiable predictions that independent data could test. This simulation meets the first three criteria. It does not yet meet the fourth — not because the predictions are not falsifiable, but because the empirical measurement program to test them does not yet exist. That measurement program is what the AMC research extension is designed to produce.

What Would Validate or Falsify the Model

▶

The simulation's primary prediction is: the trust vector between the scientific institution and national governance has been declining since approximately 2000 and is currently below the coordination threshold. Four empirical measures would test this:

1
Expert engagement rates
Frequency with which IPCC authors participate in national policy deliberations versus international treaty processes. If Γ(sci↔gov) < Γ(sci↔treaty), engagement should be lower in national processes — which is testable from attendance records and participation logs.
2
Policy responsiveness analysis
Correlation across jurisdictions and time periods between improvements in IPCC certainty levels and changes in national emissions policy. If the trust vector has been declining, this correlation should be weak and weakening — consistent with observed patterns but not formally tested against the simulation's specific trajectory.
3
Morphic suppression proxies
Surveys of the climate science community measuring self-editing frequency, strategic qualification of findings, and avoidance of alarming projections. The model predicts morphic suppression building in the scientific institution across 2015–2023, accelerating after step 5 (~2020) as the treaty trust vector also falls below threshold.
4
Trust vector direct measurement
Political scientists and network analysts measuring formal and informal communication pathways between IPCC institutions and national executive governance across jurisdictions — tracking whether pathways have become more or less dense, more or less reciprocal, and more or less effective in transmitting scientific consensus into policy deliberation.
