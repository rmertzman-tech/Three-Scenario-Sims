Chapter 2 showed you the suppression gap from the outside. You were the analyst. The telescope was pointed at an institution — NASA's launch decision architecture in January 1986. What you measured was structural: the distance between what Roger Boisjoly knew and what reached the launch decision.

The Precise Structural Difference

Chapter 2's suppression gap is an institutional measure — the distance between what exists in a system and what reaches its decisions. Chapter 3's morphic fidelity is an identity measure — the ratio of what remains coherent to what has been eroded under suppression. Both are structural. Neither is about individual virtue.

§2 — Bishop Hanna's PRF

Chapter 2 developed Boisjoly's PRF to explain why his configuration made him structurally positioned to see what the NASA architecture suppressed. Hanna's PRF serves a different structural purpose: it explains why his configuration made his identity structurally resistant to the suppression architecture his captors designed.

The key structural insight: his captors needed to accomplish one thing — get Hanna to say the words of conversion. The obstacle was not physical. The obstacle was his generative model. His beliefs, rules, and ontology together constituted a predictive architecture that processed the death threat in a way that made compliance more catastrophically dissonant than death.

§3 — The Thermodynamic Universe

Before we can read what the simulation measures, we need to understand the universe it is modelling — specifically what it means to be a living, thinking, choosing being inside a cosmos that trends naturally toward disorder.

The Second Law and Identity

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that in any closed system, entropy — disorder, randomness, the degradation of organized structure — tends to increase over time. Ice melts. Buildings crumble. Complex organizations dissolve.

A living human being is a local, temporary, energy-expensive exception to this trend. You are a low-entropy structure in a high-entropy universe. Every moment you are alive, your body is consuming energy to maintain the organized complexity that makes you you. Identity coherence is not a fixed possession — it is an ongoing thermodynamic project requiring continuous energy expenditure.

"You have approximately 37 trillion cells, each consuming energy every second to maintain its organized state. Your brain alone uses about 20% of your body's energy budget — roughly 12 watts, continuously — to maintain the electrochemical patterns that constitute your thoughts, memories, and sense of self. The idea that 'just existing' is effortless is a biological illusion. Existence is expensive. Identity is even more expensive." — Chapter 3, Ethics Beyond Boundaries

The Sapolsky Gate — Bottom-Up Causality

Robert Sapolsky's research establishes that chronic stress measurably degrades the prefrontal cortex — the brain region responsible for deliberate reasoning. The simulation models this through the Sapolsky gate: as stress rises past approximately 0.65, C_PFC collapses and the agent is pushed from MANUAL mode (deliberate reasoning) into AUTOMATIC mode (reactive, survival responses). Compliance — conversion — was what Hanna's captors were designing for.

The Sapolsky Gate — What It Means for Ethics

If chronic stress degrades the capacity for deliberate moral reasoning, then the conditions under which people make ethical decisions matter — not just morally, but biologically. A person carrying accumulated trauma has a literally different capacity for ethical deliberation than a person with adequate rest and safety. This is not an excuse for harmful choices. It is a structural fact about what ethics costs.

Top-Down Causality — The Identity Kernel as Constraint

But Sapolsky does not tell the whole story. What we call 'identity' is neurologically a set of high-precision priors: predictions about 'who I am' that are so confident, so deeply established, that they literally suppress alternative neural pathways through inhibitory synaptic connections.

This is top-down causality: the Identity Kernel generates predictions so confident that they constrain which biological pathways activate. The psychology is implemented by the biology at a different organizational level, and it operates causally downward onto the biology beneath it.

Hanna's 34-year-deep formation generated predictions of extraordinary precision — "I am a priest," "my faith is non-negotiable," "death is less catastrophic than betrayal" — that were physically suppressing the alternative biological pathways even under maximum biological pressure. The result that should be impossible according to a purely bottom-up account: his deliberate reasoning capacity held.

§5 — BioBond and the Extended Mind

Chapter 1 established Andy Clark's extended mind thesis: the cognitive boundary is not at the skull. Your PRF is scaffolded by the community structures, institutional environments, and relationships you inhabit. When Hanna's captors severed his connection to his Church community, his family, and his congregation, they were not merely isolating him emotionally. They were dismantling part of his cognitive system.

What BioBond Actually Provides

In the simulation, BioBond models the restoration of one component of that distributed cognitive system. The Church's international network did not reduce the entropy of the captivity environment — it did not change the block fields. What it maintained was the external cognitive scaffolding that kept Hanna's generative model stable under conditions designed to destabilize it.

Run 1 vs Run 2 — The Structural Claim

Both simulation runs have identical suppression conditions — same entropy (0.88), same stress (0.76), same block fields. The only structural difference: BioBond activates at step 4 in Run 2, representing the moment the Church network mobilized (Pope Benedict XVI praying publicly, Church leaders across multiple countries organizing the ransom).

The fidelity improvement between Run 1 and Run 2 is the numerical measure of what Clark's extended mind thesis predicts: severing the external component of a distributed cognitive system degrades the coherence of the whole; restoring it partially restores that coherence.

Not Emotional Support — Structural Scaffolding

"Emotional comfort" is the folk-psychology description of what BioBond provides structurally. The simulation models the structural effect: the external network maintains the precision weighting on the agent's identity priors, which keeps the generative model stable and prevents the morphic suppression accumulation that would occur without it.

The Privilege Dimension

Bishop Saad's Church mobilized on his behalf: a global organization with centuries of infrastructure directed at maintaining one person's capacity to survive. Most people facing institutional suppression have far less robust BioBond structures. The simulation predicts that without institutional support, λ Raw drops and morphic suppression rises. The implication is structural, not moral: people with less BioBond are operating under higher effective entropy, in conditions more likely to produce the kind of graduated learned self-censorship that morphic suppression measures.

His resilience was individual virtue and structural enablement simultaneously. The unequal distribution of BioBond structures is itself an ethical question.
