Chapter 6 established that presenting one forecast as the answer is a moral choice — it suppresses the genuine uncertainty distribution that decision-makers need. Tim Palmer’s ensemble method corrected this by routing the full distribution toward decisions rather than collapsing it before it arrived.

Chapter 7 takes the logic one step further. Palmer asked: what if the model is uncertain? Farmer asks: what if the model is looking at the wrong thing entirely? Specifically — what if the model is looking at an average person who does not exist, instead of the actual heterogeneous people who do?

The Structural Parallel

Palmer’s single-model forecast collapses a distribution of outcomes into one track and calls it the forecast. Farmer’s representative agent collapses a distribution of agents into one average and calls it the economy. Both errors have the same shape: replacing a genuine distribution with a false singleton. Both have the same consequence: the people in the tails of the distribution become invisible to decisions that affect them.

Three Chapters, One Argument

  • Chapter 2 (Boisjoly): High capability can exist in an institution and still not reach decisions if the suppression gap is near-total. The capability was present. The architecture failed it.
  • Chapter 6 (Palmer): Presenting one forecast as the answer suppresses the uncertainty distribution. The honest representation requires showing the distribution, not collapsing it to a point.
  • Chapter 7 (Farmer): Modeling the average agent suppresses the heterogeneity distribution. The honest representation requires seeing each agent’s actual state, not averaging them away.

All three are the same error at different scales. All three have the same ethical consequence.

§2 — The Representative Agent: The Average Person Who Does Not Exist

For most of the twentieth century, mainstream economics built its models on a single assumption so embedded it was rarely stated: rational homogeneity. Economic agents — firms, households, individuals — are interchangeable. They have the same information, the same decision-making processes, the same capacity to optimize. When you run the model, you run it on one representative firm and one representative household.

This assumption made the mathematics tractable. It also made the most important information structurally invisible: how specific agents at specific structural positions with specific constraint profiles actually behave under stress.

What the Representative Agent Cannot See

Imagine you are designing a public health policy. You model the Average Resident — average income, average savings, average health, average family situation, average access to institutional support. Your policy is designed for this person.

The problem: the Average Resident does not exist. Not a single actual person has exactly average values simultaneously across all dimensions. The policy designed for the average will work for people near average and systematically fail people who are furthest from it — who are typically the most vulnerable.

This Is Not a Technical Limitation

The representative agent assumption was not adopted because economists believed it was accurate. It was adopted because it made the equations solvable. The choice to model the average was a choice about what kind of information the model would be capable of receiving. A model built around the average cannot receive the signal that non-average agents generate. Their specific constraints, their network positions, their resilience margins — all of it is averaged away before the model runs.

“The workhorse models of macroeconomics can’t easily absorb the well-known insights of behavioural economics. But numerical approximation allows us to model economic actors more realistically than a single rational Homo economicus representative agent. Systems of heterogeneous competing agents are intrinsically chaotic.” — J. Doyne Farmer, Making Sense of Chaos (2024)

In March 2020, with COVID-19 spreading rapidly and governments scrambling to understand what lockdowns would do to their economies, Farmer’s Oxford team built a model that differed from the representative agent framework in one crucial way: instead of one average firm, they modeled a representative firm in every sector of the economy — each with its actual constraints.

The Three-Factor Rule

A firm that does not have the workers it needs cannot produce. A firm that does not have the inputs it needs cannot produce. A firm that does not have demand for its product cannot produce. Output falls in proportion to whichever factor is limiting.

This rule sounds simple. It was actually a radical departure from equilibrium economics. Standard models assumed firms would rationally substitute and adjust to find an optimum. Farmer’s model assumed firms face real structural limiting factors that cannot be wished away by rational optimization. A restaurant that cannot seat customers cannot substitute remote revenue. The constraint is real.

The Data That Made It Possible

To build the prediction, Farmer’s team needed something standard models did not use: actual heterogeneous data about actual agents. They used Bureau of Labor Statistics occupational tables showing how physically close together workers in each occupation typically work. If a job required workers closer than two meters, the model assumed lockdown made it impossible. Not a guess about human behavior — a structural observation about specific jobs in specific sectors.

They then tracked how each sector’s output failure cascaded into input failures for every sector that depended on it. The cascade proceeded through the actual network of economic dependencies, not through the theoretical adjustments of a rational representative agent.

The Macrocosm Vision

Farmer’s current project, Macrocosm, aims to do for economic planning what Google Maps did for traffic planning — providing anyone with an economic question an intelligent, data-grounded answer. The key insight: Google Maps works because it tracks actual vehicles at actual locations. A traffic model using the Average Vehicle at the Average Location would be systematically wrong the moment actual traffic diverged from the average — which is always.

From Firms to People

What Farmer discovered about firms applies with equal force to people inside institutions. Every firm in his model needed labor access, input access, and demand to maintain constructive capacity. Remove any one and output fell to zero for that agent at that moment — not to a new equilibrium, but to zero.

Every person inside an institution has analogous constructor requirements. They need cognitive access (stress below the Sapolsky threshold), information access (accurate evidence reaching them), and contribution routing (their observations reaching decisions). Remove any one and their ethical functioning degrades — not because they changed, but because the architecture changed. This is the simulation you have been running since Chapter 2.

§4 — The Social Block Field: What Farmer Would Need to Know About You

When you run the simulation, you set parameters called block fields. You specify whether the community block is positive or negative, whether the fairness block is above threshold, what the BioBond value is. In this section, we make those parameters visible as features of actual social environments — not simulation abstractions.

A social block is any stable feature of a person’s relational and institutional environment that consistently routes their experience in a particular direction. A positive block supports constructor capacity: it routes accurate information toward the person, routes their contributions toward decisions, and provides the relational conditions that maintain their functioning under stress. A negative block drains constructor capacity.

Blocks Are Not Events

A single bad day is an event. A manager who consistently dismisses your observations, an institution that systematically routes decisions around the people closest to the problem, a work environment where chronic understaffing means everyone operates above the Sapolsky threshold continuously — these are blocks. They shape every interaction within them, not just the dramatic ones.

The Granular Data Requirement

This is what Farmer meant when he argued that complexity economics requires highly granular data that accurately captures heterogeneity. The granular data the simulation requires is not demographic. It is structural. It is the actual configuration of the block field in which each agent is embedded.

Farmer’s COVID model needed to know each sector’s specific constraints. The simulation needs to know each agent’s specific block field configuration. The ethical obligation that follows: an institution that wants to make good decisions about its people needs to know their actual block field states — not the average.

§6 — The Rawlsian Test: Does Good Architecture Help Everyone?

Run 3 of the Farmer simulation applied what John Rawls called the difference principle directly: institutional arrangements are just only if they benefit the least advantaged members. The simulation asks this question computationally — does the well-designed heterogeneous architecture from Run 2 produce good outcomes for the most structurally constrained agents?

What the Simulation Shows

The Capability Gap

Run 3 uses the same good architecture as Run 2 — same diverse worldviews, same positive block fields, same empirical-calibrated evidence routing. The difference: base_entropy 0.72 and stress 0.64, modeling agents whose resilience margin has already been depleted.

The simulation’s finding: the heterogeneous architecture does better for these agents than the representative architecture would. But it does not close the gap. A positive community block field does less for an agent already in cascade failure than for one with resources to engage with that support. Good architecture moves the floor. It does not eliminate the capability gap without structural investment in the agents themselves.

Farmer’s COVID Parallel

In Farmer’s COVID model, thin-buffer firms at critical network nodes failed even when the broader economy recovered — because their cash reserves were already depleted when the shock arrived. The cascade failure was structural: one additional disruption beyond what the resilience margin could absorb triggered full collapse, not a new equilibrium.

The human equivalent: a person who has been operating in a high-entropy environment for months, whose BioBond structures are thin, who has been above the Sapolsky threshold continuously — is not a less capable person. They are a person whose resilience margin has been depleted by the institutional environment they have been operating in. One additional shock cascades what might have been recoverable into full system failure.

The Complete Ethical Obligation

What Rawls adds to Farmer: it is not enough to build a heterogeneous architecture that can see all agents accurately. Justice requires that the architecture produce the best possible outcomes for the agents who are furthest from the average — the ones with the thinnest resilience margins, the most depleted BioBond structures, the highest chronic stress. This means not just removing negative blocks but actively investing in the structural conditions that would allow the most constrained agents to access what the architecture makes available.

What Farmer adds to Rawls: you cannot target this investment if you cannot see the distribution. The representative agent model makes the least advantaged invisible by averaging them away. A Rawlsian institution must first build the Farmer architecture — the capacity to see actual heterogeneous agents — before it can design interventions that maximize the minimum.

The Cooperative Information Requirement

Farmer’s Macrocosm project needs actual firm data to work. The same is true inside institutions: the block field data that would reveal an organization’s suppression gap — which agents are below threshold, which network nodes are in cascade risk — currently exists in fragments, distributed across the people who experience it. It is not shared because institutions that need it most have often built architectures that make sharing dangerous.

This is the deepest structural obligation: not merely to communicate honestly, but to build architectures that make honesty safe and actionable — that route honest information about actual agent states toward decisions, rather than filtering it through authority-weighted channels that transform signal into noise.

Looking Ahead: Chapter 8 — The Simulated History of Ethics

You have now run eight simulations across six cases. Every run was a test of something a philosopher discovered centuries ago — and often also a test of what they failed to see.

Aristotle discovered that character is infrastructure: virtuous practice requires institutional conditions that support it. The simulation measures exactly what he was measuring, with a name for the mechanism he lacked: block fields and BioBond are the computational version of Aristotle’s habituation conditions.

Kant discovered that rational agency presupposes intact deliberative capacity. The Sapolsky threshold is the simulation’s measurement of exactly what Kant assumed was always present.

Rawls discovered that justice requires maximizing the minimum — Run 3 is the Rawlsian test made computational. Chapter 8 will show you all eight stations of this history, and where the simulation connects to each.
