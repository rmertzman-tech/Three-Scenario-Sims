Where Ethics Begins

γνῶθι σεαυτόν
Know thyself
Inscription at the Oracle of Delphi · The starting point Socrates chose for all serious philosophical inquiry

This inscription was carved above the entrance to the most consulted oracle in the ancient Greek world. Socrates treated it as the most important philosophical instruction that existed — not because self-knowledge was the endpoint of ethical inquiry, but because it was the precondition. Before you can reason well about what you should do, you must be able to see the framework through which you are doing the reasoning.

What Socrates understood that most ethics courses skip

You are already operating from an ethical framework. Right now, before this course begins, you have beliefs about what is morally real, rules you follow even when no one requires it, things you experience as authentic versus performed, and a sense of where your own development is headed. You have been using this framework your entire life. You just have not had precise language for it.

This course begins by giving you that language — and then showing you what your specific framework sees with particular clarity, what it characteristically misses, and what the theoretical vocabulary of 2,500 years of moral philosophy was trying to capture that your framework was already pointing toward.

This is the opposite sequence from most ethics courses, which introduce theories first and then apply them to cases. Here the sequence is: discover your framework first, then encounter the theories as names for dimensions of moral reality you were already navigating.

"The unexamined life is not worth living."
— Socrates, Apology 38a · spoken at his trial, 399 BCE

Socrates was convicted and executed for teaching Athenian citizens to examine their lives. He accepted the verdict rather than stop the practice. The self-knowledge he was pursuing was not private introspection — it was the structural precondition for serious reasoning about how to live alongside other people in a just community.

The next section introduces the four great ethical theories — not as competing correct answers, but as four directions in which moral reality extends. Then you will complete the PRF-22, which identifies which direction your own lens is most naturally oriented toward.

Four Directions in Which Moral Reality Extends

For 2,500 years, moral philosophers have been trying to articulate what moral reality is and what it demands of us. They have not agreed. But the disagreement is not arbitrary — it has a structure. Each major tradition is sensitive to a genuine dimension of moral experience that the others see less clearly. Together, they are four instruments, each calibrated for a different frequency of moral life.

Select any card to explore that theory. Notice which one feels most immediately recognizable — that recognition is your first piece of self-knowledge for today.

🏛️
Virtue Ethics
"Who are you becoming?"
Aristotle · MacIntyre · Foot. Character is the foundation. The stable dispositions — courage, justice, honesty, practical wisdom — that make a person admirable and a life worth living. What the community must provide for virtue to be possible.
⚖️
Deontology
"What is your duty?"
Kant · Korsgaard · O'Neill. Rational agency carries inherent dignity. There are categorical limits — things you cannot do to persons regardless of the outcome they would produce. Every person is an end in themselves, never merely a means.
📊
Consequentialism
"What does this produce?"
Mill · Bentham · Singer. Outcomes for sentient beings are morally fundamental. The right action produces the greatest good across all those affected, calculated impartially. Results matter — and they matter equally regardless of whose they are.
🤝
Care Ethics
"Who are you in relation with?"
Noddings · Gilligan · Held. The relational network of care and dependency is morally primary. Attentiveness to the particular other, responsiveness to vulnerability. The web of mutual recognition within which any justice worth having becomes possible.

Why four? And why can't we just pick the right one?

Each theory sees something real. Each also generates characteristic anomalies — observations it can accommodate only through increasingly complex adjustments — at the scale of institutional life. Virtue ethics cannot easily explain why people of apparently good character produce harmful institutions. Deontology cannot easily explain why fulfilled individual duties still produce structural violations no individual chose. Consequentialism cannot easily see the structural distortions that make individual utility calculations systematically wrong in organizations. Care ethics cannot easily diagnose the institutional designs that sever relational networks at scale.

This course does not ask you to choose one. It shows you what all four see — and then shows you the reference frame from which all four become more coherent simultaneously.

The ethics journal — your companion across every unit

Beginning in the next section and continuing throughout the course, you will maintain an ethics journal. It is not a diary. It is the written form of the Socratic examined life — the record of your self-knowledge in dialogue with everything the course shows you. The final creative architecture exercise (Unit 7) draws directly from it. The journal is the most important document you will produce in this course.

📝 Entry 1d: The Socratic Question

Socrates' method was to ask careful questions until he reached the foundations of a belief — and then keep asking. Choose one belief you hold about ethics — something you are fairly confident is true about what is right or wrong. Ask: why do I believe this? What does my belief rest on? If someone asked me to justify the foundation, what would I say? Write until you reach something you cannot justify further.
