Overview — The Palmer-Farmer Parallel

Chapter 6 established that single-model forecasting creates a suppression gap by compressing a genuine probability distribution to false certainty. Palmer's ensemble approach closed that gap by reporting the distribution as the primary output.

Chapter 7 makes the same argument about economics. Standard macroeconomic models use a representative agent — one idealized rational actor standing in for the entire economy. A lockdown's economic impact modeled this way looks like one person's decision. It does not look like a restaurant worker, a hospital nurse, a remote software engineer, and a manufacturing supply chain manager — all experiencing the same lockdown in categorically different ways.

The structural parallel is precise:

  • Palmer's single-model forecast compresses a genuine probability distribution to one track → forecasting suppression gap
  • Standard economics compresses a genuine population distribution to one representative agent → economic suppression gap
  • In both cases, the populations most harmed by the events being modeled are precisely the populations most invisible to the model

The Representative Agent — What It Is and What It Hides

What Standard Models Assume

Standard macroeconomic models are built around a representative agent: a single, idealized, rational household that owns all the firms in the economy, has perfect foresight about future prices and interest rates, and makes optimal decisions in every period. This is not a description of any real household. It is a simplification that made complex mathematics tractable before heterogeneous agent computation became feasible.

The Three Things Representative Agent Models Cannot See

  • Supply chain topology: which firms depend on which other firms, in what order closures cascade, which communities lose multiple income sources simultaneously.
  • Distributional impacts: the same shock hits different households with different severity. The average impact can be modest even when the worst impacts are catastrophic.
  • Behavioral heterogeneity: people with different resources respond differently to the same policy. The representative agent responds optimally. Agents without savings, without credit access, and without work flexibility cannot respond optimally — and policy designed for the optimal responder fails them.
"When COVID hit, the standard models said roughly: 'large shock, GDP falls sharply, recovery gradual.' What they could not say was: which supply chains collapse first, which workers face unemployment immediately, where the cascading effects hit hardest, and which communities need support this week versus next quarter. The representative agent experiences the average of all of this. Nobody in the actual economy experiences the average." Doyne Farmer, Institute for New Economic Thinking, 2020

Farmer and COVID — When Heterogeneity Saved Lives

The Modeling Problem in March 2020

When COVID-19 struck, governments needed to make rapid decisions about sector closures, support payments, and fiscal commitments. Standard macroeconomic models produced aggregate GDP projections. They could not produce what decision-makers actually needed: which sectors would face cash-flow collapse in week 2, which supply chains would cascade, and which households would be in crisis before the first support payment arrived.

What Farmer's Model Did Differently

  • Supply-chain mapping: each firm in the model had specific input dependencies and output buyers. When restaurants closed, the food distributors and produce suppliers who depended on restaurant revenue faced immediate cash-flow problems — visible in the model, invisible to aggregate GDP forecasting.
  • Household vulnerability gradients: households were modeled with their actual income sources, savings buffers, and sector employment. A household dependent on hospitality income with zero savings was in crisis in week 1. A household with accumulated savings and remote-compatible employment faced a temporary inconvenience. Same lockdown, categorically different impact.
  • Policy sequencing: the model showed that the timing of support mattered as much as the total amount. Early cash transfer to zero-income households prevented cascading defaults. Delayed support after cascades had already occurred cost significantly more to address.

Synthesis & Transfer — The Invisible Population in Your World

The Farmer Principle Beyond Economics

The representative agent problem is not specific to economics. Every institution that models populations for policy purposes faces the same choice: represent the population as a homogeneous agent — averaged characteristics, assumed rationality, standard behavior — or retain the heterogeneity that makes distributional analysis possible.

Healthcare — The Representative Patient

Clinical trials have historically used populations with specific demographic profiles (white, male, middle-aged, no comorbidities) as the representative patient. Treatment recommendations calibrated to this average are systematically less accurate for populations not represented. The drug that works for the representative patient may require different dosing, have different adverse effects, or be contraindicated for patients outside the trial's demographic band. Policy designed for the representative patient fails patients at the distribution's edges.

Education — The Representative Student

Standardized testing policy is calibrated to the average student whose performance is stable across test conditions, who has access to preparation resources, and whose background knowledge matches the test's assumed knowledge base. The distributional question — what happens to the 15% of students whose performance is most sensitive to test-specific factors — rarely reaches policy decisions calibrated to average pass rates and average score gaps.

Public Health — The Representative Household

Epidemiological models use population-average transmission rates, immunity profiles, and behavioral responses. These systematically underestimate the severity of outbreaks in high-density, low-income communities where household size, public transit dependence, and inability to work from home produce transmission rates far above the population average. The policy designed for the representative agent — 'stay home if symptomatic, work from home if possible' — requires resources that a substantial fraction of the actual population cannot access.

The Farmer Principle — Stated Generally

In any institution that manages complex populations through aggregate models: the populations most harmed by the events being modeled are systematically the populations most invisible to the model. The ethical obligation runs in both directions: use heterogeneous models that make all populations visible AND design policies calibrated to the distribution rather than the mean — specifically calibrated to close the gap for the agents at the bottom of the distribution.

This is Rawls' difference principle applied to modeling methodology: the choice of model architecture is itself an ethical choice with distributive consequences.

Looking Ahead: Chapter 8 — The Simulated History of Ethics

Chapters 6 and 7 completed the methodology argument: you have been practicing ensemble thinking and heterogeneous agent analysis all semester. Those methods have names in the philosophical tradition. Aristotle was doing something structurally equivalent when he described virtue as a stable disposition formed by habituation — he was modeling the agent's capacity as a function of their institutional environment, which is what λ Raw measures.

Kant was doing something structurally equivalent when he argued that every rational agent must be treated as an end in themselves — he was arguing that the suppression gap is a Kantian violation, that an architecture that systematically routes around agents' rational contributions is treating them as means rather than ends. Rawls' difference principle is a formal statement of the Run 3 question: design the institution to maximize the minimum λ Raw, not the average. Chapter 8 will make these connections explicit.
