The 1.4-Point Gap to Functional State

SPC is 1.4 FHRI_max points below the “Functional / Mission-Aligned” threshold (60.0). The IPS framework does not tell you whether to close that gap. It tells you where to look: the three parameters that constitute the extraction floor (ε, φ, σ) and the two that constitute the routing ceiling (ρ, μ). A 1.4-point improvement requires a very small structural shift in one or more of those parameters — not a character transformation, not a budget miracle. A measurable, addressable governance adjustment.

To close the gap
Reduce ε by 0.01 (shift 1% of administrative spend to instruction), OR reduce φ by 0.008 (reduce median student debt relative to earnings by improving completion pathways), OR reduce μ by 0.01 (tighten the enrollment-growth vs. completion-rate alignment). Any one of these alone would push FHRI_max above 60.
What it is not
A judgment that SPC faculty, staff, or administrators are not working hard enough. FHRI_max measures the structural ceiling imposed by institutional architecture — not the effort or dedication of the individuals inside it. The most dedicated SPC instructor operates within the same structural constraints as every other SPC employee. That is what makes the structural ceiling a governance question rather than a personnel question.

What the 17.6-point gap means formally

The scale-free law predicts that high-ε ownership architecture produces FHRI_max suppression regardless of sector. It does not predict that all suppression reaches the same floor. The exit option — the ability to leave an institution even at high personal cost — functions as a structural floor that prevents the full collapse seen in captive-population institutions.

When a student at the University of Phoenix accumulates $42,000 in debt for a credential that produces $35,200 in median earnings, they bear enormous φ. But they can leave. When an incarcerated person at a GEO Group facility experiences 89% of coordination capacity absorbed before it reaches programming decisions, they cannot leave. The exit option modulates how low the FHRI_max ceiling can fall, not whether it falls.

This is not a defense of the University of Phoenix. FHRI_max = 26.9 means 73.1% of coordination capacity is structurally absorbed before it reaches the student. Median debt $42,000. Median earnings $35,200. 3-year repayment rate 41%. The Interpretant replacement — from “education as pathway to knowledge and mobility” to “enrollment as revenue unit” — is real and documented. The distinction is: the harm is severe AND it is bounded by the exit option. The harm in captive-population sectors is severe AND it is unbounded.

The testable prediction

If the exit-option modulator hypothesis is correct, then: within higher education, institutions where the effective exit cost is highest — credits that do not transfer, debt already incurred at enrollment, employer-sponsored tuition tied to specific institutions — should have lower FHRI_max than institutions where exit is easier. This is a falsifiable prediction derivable from the Scorecard data. The next phase of this research will test it.

IPS Parameters: How the Numbers Were Derived

Each parameter is derived from a specific IPEDS or College Scorecard data field. The derivation is documented so any researcher can replicate the computation with an API key and a spreadsheet. These are not theoretical estimates — they are computed from the same public data the U.S. Department of Education uses to assess institutional performance.
ε (EXTRACTION) — IPEDS Finance: Instructional vs. Administrative Expenditure
ε = 1 − (instructional spend per FTE / total educational spend per FTE). For SPC: instructional spend $5,840 / total $8,050 = 72.5% reaching instruction = ε 0.275. For University of Phoenix: instructional spend $2,180 / total $8,120 = 26.8% reaching instruction = ε 0.732. The ratio of admin-to-instruction spending is the most direct IPEDS proxy for the extraction rate.
φ (ENTROPY) — College Scorecard: Median Debt / Median Earnings
φ = normalized(median debt at completion / median earnings 6 years post-enrollment). SPC: $8,500 / $32,800 = 0.26 ratio = φ 0.011 (near zero harm). University of Phoenix: $42,000 / $35,200 = 1.19 ratio = φ 0.305 (significant harm). The debt-to-earnings ratio is the College Scorecard’s own primary metric for institutional financial harm to students.
ρ (REGULATORY BURDEN) — IPEDS: Admin fraction + Federal loan dependency
ρ = 0.70 × (admin spend / total spend) + 0.30 × (federal loan rate − 0.20). This captures both the internal compliance overhead and the external Title IV dependency that drives compliance theater. Institutions that derive most of their revenue from federal student loans have strong incentives to maintain accreditation compliance regardless of educational quality — the regulatory burden generates Sign-level compliance without Interpretant alignment.
σ (MORPHIC RESIDUE) — IPEDS: Tuition inflation vs. CPI + repayment deficit
σ = 0.60 × (tuition inflation excess over CPI) + 0.40 × (repayment deficit). Cumulative CPI 2010–2023 = 1.42×. Institutions that raised tuition significantly faster than CPI while maintaining enrollment normalized the practice. Each year of tuition inflation exceeding CPI without triggering enrollment collapse added to σ. The 3-year repayment rate deficit from the 85% healthy benchmark captures whether the income normalizing the deviance was actually sustainable.
μ (MISALIGNMENT) — Scorecard: Enrollment growth vs. completion rate trajectory
μ = 0.50 × (enrollment growth 5yr − completion rate change 5yr) + 0.50 × completer indifference. The first component measures whether the institution was rewarded for growing enrollment regardless of completion outcomes — the originate-to-distribute parallel in higher education. The second measures whether the institution was structurally indifferent to who completed (small gap between completer and non-completer debt = institution continues extracting revenue regardless of whether students finish).

P-SAGE: The Formal Structural Analysis

The IPS numbers ground the P-SAGE diagnosis. The three Perception questions applied to higher education produce a formal finding about who accessed the channel, when, and through which instrument — and where the formal responsible party sits.
SIGN (Firstness)
INTACT
Colleges operate. Degrees are awarded. Accreditation is maintained. Students enroll. Classes meet. The ACA 100% parallel: SACSCOC accreditation scores do not measure FHRI_max. High accreditation scores are consistent with FHRI_max = 26.9.
SIGNAL (Secondness)
ACCESSED — 1992
The tuition revenue allocation channel. P-adic access achieved through the 1992 HEA amendments expanding Title IV eligibility to for-profit institutions. This placed for-profit entities inside the federal student loan cluster previously restricted to non-profit and public institutions.
INTERPRETANT (Thirdness)
REPLACED
Original: tuition = investment in educational capacity producing knowledge and economic mobility.
Replaced: enrollment = revenue unit serviced by federal loan guarantees, with risk externalized onto students and taxpayers.
P-step output — formally derived responsible party:

1. Original Interpretant: tuition = investment in educational capacity.
2. Replaced Interpretant: enrollment = revenue extraction vehicle (ε 0.73, FHRI_max 26.9).
3. Signal channel: tuition revenue allocation — what tuition payments are used for once received.
4. P-adic access mechanism: 1992 Higher Education Act amendments + Bennett Hypothesis (federal loan availability removed the market constraint on tuition pricing, enabling the Interpretant replacement to be enacted without the competitive pressure that would otherwise limit it).

Formally derived responsible party: not the students who borrowed, not the faculty who taught, not the administrators who enforced enrollment targets. The ownership architecture that achieved P-adic proximity to the tuition revenue channel through regulatory access and executed Interpretant replacement from educational-investment to extraction-vehicle.

Where SPC sits in this analysis

SPC’s low ε (0.275) means the Interpretant replacement has not propagated into SPC’s institutional architecture. The channel that was accessed in 1992 is present in all Title IV institutions — including SPC. But the degree to which an institution uses that access to replace the Interpretant depends on ownership structure, governance, and institutional purpose. SPC’s public mission, state funding accountability, and open-access design have maintained a lower ε than the for-profit sector that most aggressively enacted the replacement. The telescope on SPC shows an institution that has resisted the full Interpretant replacement — not perfectly, but measurably and significantly.
