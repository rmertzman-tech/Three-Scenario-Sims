The Telescope Turned Inward

From Adam Smith’s Moral Sentiments through mathematical supply and demand to the UCP — how economics lost sight of the human being, and what it takes to find them again

Adam Smith Had Two Books — and We Forgot the First One

The founding text of economics is not The Wealth of Nations. It is The Theory of Moral Sentiments, published in 1759 — seventeen years earlier. Smith spent more of his life revising it. He considered it the more fundamental work. Understanding why he was right is the key to understanding what the UCP is actually doing.

The Theory of Moral Sentiments begins with an observation that sounds simple but carries enormous weight: human beings are not isolated maximizers. We are constitutively social creatures whose capacity to make judgments — including economic judgments — depends on our ability to imaginatively inhabit the perspectives of others. Smith called this faculty sympathy, and he argued that it is the foundation not only of ethics but of all stable social coordination, including markets.

The invisible hand Smith described in Wealth of Nations was never meant to operate in a moral vacuum. It was the mechanism by which individually pursued interests, within a social fabric of sympathy and shared norms, could produce collectively beneficial outcomes. The sympathy of Moral Sentiments was the operating condition the market mechanism presupposed. Without it, the hand is not invisible — it is absent.

Smith’s invisible hand only works inside a society whose members can imaginatively inhabit each other’s situations.
Remove the sympathy, and you remove the hand.

This is not a minor historical footnote. It is the root of the central problem that has haunted economics ever since: the discipline formalized the market mechanism while treating the moral and social conditions that make it function as exogenous — as given background rather than as variables that markets themselves can erode or restore. The UCP makes those conditions endogenous: FHRI_max, PRF, and the IPS parameters are precisely the variables that standard economics leaves off the model.

1759
The Theory of Moral Sentiments
Smith establishes sympathy — the capacity to imaginatively inhabit another’s perspective — as the foundation of social coordination. Markets are embedded in moral communities. The invisible hand presupposes this embedding.
1776
The Wealth of Nations
Smith formalizes the market mechanism: division of labor, comparative advantage, the price system as information aggregator. Implicitly assumes the moral fabric of Moral Sentiments as background. Economics begins here for most students — but this is book two, not book one.
1870s
The Marginalist Revolution
Jevons, Walras, Marshall convert political economy into mathematical economics. Supply and demand become curves. Agents become representative utility-maximizers. The moral fabric drops out of the model because it cannot be expressed as an equation.
1950s–70s
Behavioral economics & bounded rationality
Simon, Kahneman, Tversky demonstrate that real agents systematically violate the rationality assumptions. Heuristics, loss aversion, framing effects are not noise — they are the dominant features of actual human economic decision-making.
1980s–present
Complexity economics (Farmer, Santa Fe)
The economy as a complex adaptive system of heterogeneous agents. Equilibrium is an occasional attractor, not the natural state. Capital allocation is itself a complex adaptive process shaped by institutional arrangements and information asymmetries.
UCP V5.0
The epistemic telescope
The UCP makes visible what the price signal cannot carry: heterogeneous agents (PRF, Identity Kernel), substrate costs (φ), and the structural ceiling on coordination capacity (FHRI_max). It does not replace mathematical economics. It extends its interpretive reach into the dimensions it currently cannot see.

The Mathematical Turn: What Was Gained and What Was Lost

The gains from mathematical formalization were real. Predictions could be tested. Policies could be compared. Effects could be measured with precision that moral philosophy could not match. But the formalization required a simplification that Smith never made — and the simplification cost the model its most important subject matter.

To write supply and demand as curves, you need agents whose behavior can be aggregated — agents who are identical in their decision-making structure, fully informed, and capable of consistent preference maximization. This is the representative agent: a single decision-maker whose behavior stands in for an entire population.

The representative agent is not merely a simplification. It is a category error. Real markets are populated by agents who are heterogeneous in their information, their cognitive capacity, their power relative to other actors, and their position in institutional hierarchies. The price signal can aggregate their behavior into an equilibrium, but it cannot represent the distribution of who captures value, who bears cost, or how the coordination mechanism shapes the agents who participate in it over time.

Adam Smith’s agents (1759–1776)
Heterogeneous in their sympathies, histories, power relations, and cognitive capacities. Markets are embedded in moral communities. Norms constrain what can be bought, sold, or extracted. The invisible hand presupposes a background of shared sentiments that no individual agent controls.
The mathematical representative agent (1870s–present)
Identical to all other agents in decision structure. Markets are information aggregation mechanisms operating on price signals alone. Agent psychology is captured entirely by a utility function. Power differentials, cognitive limitations, and moral norms are exogenous noise.

What the price signal cannot carry

•
Heterogeneous agents at different positions in institutional hierarchies, with different information access, different cognitive capacities, and different power to set the terms of exchange. The supply curve treats a homeowner facing foreclosure and an investment bank structuring CDOs as equivalent nodes in the same market.
•
Path dependence: how the history of an agent’s interactions shapes their PRF and consequently their market behavior in ways no utility function evaluated at a single moment can capture. The worker who has spent fifteen years in a high-extraction environment has a different PRF than the entering worker — and the difference is economically significant.
•
The φ problem: how market outcomes externalize substrate costs onto communities, workers, and public systems that do not appear in prices. The $19.2 trillion in household wealth destroyed by the 2008 financial crisis is not visible in any equilibrium price. It is φ that the market mechanism externalizes and cannot account for.
•
Institutional endogeneity: how the institutions through which markets operate change the agents who participate in them — extracting coordination capacity from some (FHRI_max falls) while concentrating it in others. The market equilibrium is determined by today’s agents; the agents tomorrow will be shaped by today’s market.
The price signal correctly summarizes what agents with equal information and equal power agreed to exchange.
It cannot tell you who bore the cost that did not appear in the price.
The UCP is not a replacement for mathematical economics. It is an epistemic telescope — a tool that extends interpretive reach into the dimensions of economic life that price signals cannot carry. The telescope does not invalidate the supply and demand curves. It situates them correctly: as accurate descriptions of price-mediated coordination among agents with roughly similar information and power, producing outcomes that are efficient in the narrow sense of clearing markets, but systematically incomplete in failing to account for who bears the costs that do not appear in prices.

The ΔFHRI_max Classifier: Good Disruption from Bad

The IPS framework could, if misapplied, condemn capitalism’s most productive features along with its pathologies. If P-SAGE reads Netflix as a parasite on Blockbuster, the framework is worse than useless. We need a formal criterion that distinguishes creative destruction from extractive parasitism. The ΔFHRI_max classifier provides it.

The criterion is the sign of the change in the structural ceiling on coordination capacity when one institution displaces another. This single number does significant work: it means the framework is not anti-innovation or anti-market. It endorses any transition that increases FHRI_max, regardless of how disruptive it is to incumbents.

ΔFHRI_max > 0 — PRODUCTIVE DISRUPTION (Schumpeter / Serres Type 2)
The replacing institution has a higher coordination capacity ceiling than the institution it displaces. Netflix (ε ≈ 0.15, FHRI_max ≈ 65) displaced Blockbuster (ε ≈ 0.30, FHRI_max ≈ 44). ΔFHRI_max ≈ +21. The channel was replaced by a better one. This is what the invisible hand is supposed to do. The incumbent bears φ — job losses are real, community disruption is real — but the overall coordination function improves.
ΔFHRI_max ≈ 0 — COMPETITIVE DISPLACEMENT
Two similar institutions compete. One wins. Coordination capacity is approximately unchanged. Capital has reallocated but the system’s structural ceiling is similar. Standard market competition without structural transformation. Morally neutral from the IPS perspective.
ΔFHRI_max < 0 — EXTRACTIVE PARASITISM (Serres Type 1)
The replacing institution has a lower coordination capacity ceiling. PE firm acquires nursing home: FHRI_max falls from 62.9 to 13.6. ΔFHRI_max = −49.3. The channel was replaced by one that appears to serve the same function while destroying 77% of its coordination capacity. Sign intact; Interpretant replaced; φ cascades to the populations depending on the coordination function.

The φ Question: Even Good Disruption Has Costs

ΔFHRI_max > 0 does not mean the transition is cost-free. It means the coordination function improves. But φ still lands somewhere. On whom it lands is the separate distributional question that ΔFHRI_max alone does not answer — and it is where Smith’s moral sentiments return as a practical requirement, not just a philosophical aspiration.

When Netflix displaced Blockbuster, approximately 60,000 Blockbuster employees lost jobs. The coordination capacity of film delivery increased. But the workers who bore φ were not the shareholders who captured the FHRI_max gains. ΔFHRI_max = +21 at the system level. φ was distributed asymmetrically: workers bore it; investors captured the gain.

Standard economics tracks the system-level outcome: efficiency gains, consumer surplus, capital reallocation. The price signal carries this information accurately. What the price signal cannot carry is the distributional question: who bore φ, and does the entity bearing φ have any claim on the FHRI_max gains?

Creative destruction is genuinely creative when ΔFHRI_max > 0
and the φ is borne by the actors capturing the gain.
It becomes merely destructive when the φ is externalized
onto those with no access to the gain.

True Solvency Accounting

The deepest limitation of mathematical economics is that it can only measure what appears in prices. True Solvency Accounting addresses this: φ imposed on communities, workers, and public systems appears as a liability on the firm’s balance sheet, measured by the FHRI_max degradation the firm produces in the systems it affects.

A firm that appears profitable because it has externalized its costs onto communities it does not own is not actually solvent — it has borrowed against a balance sheet belonging to others. The 2008 financial system at peak extraction was, under True Solvency Accounting, deeply insolvent: it was recording profits by externalizing φ onto nine million households that would lose their homes.

Standard accounting (what it sees)
Revenue - Costs (as priced in markets) = Profit. φ imposed on communities, workers, future generations = zero. A nursing home that cuts staffing ratios below the threshold of genuine care appears more profitable. A financial firm that packages toxic debt and sells it to pension funds appears profitable until the cascade arrives.
True Solvency Accounting (what it reveals)
Revenue - Costs (priced) - φ (FHRI_max degradation imposed on others) = True net position. A firm that appears profitable by externalizing φ is running a hidden deficit on the balance sheet of the communities it affects. This deficit will eventually be paid — either by the firm through BioBond obligations, or by the public through TARP and similar mechanisms.
The BioBond in the context of legitimate disruption. Even when ΔFHRI_max > 0, a BioBond instrument on the disruptor — indexed to the FHRI_max gain and dedicated to worker transition support — makes the creative destruction more complete. The workers bear φ in the short term, but the BioBond ensures they participate in the FHRI_max gain. This is not a subsidy to the incumbent. It is an accurate accounting of who pays for the efficiency gain — and a mechanism that routes some of the gain to the entities who bore the cost of producing it.
