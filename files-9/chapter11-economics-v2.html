Case 1: The Home Health Labor Extraction Gap

A Medicare-certified home health agency in Florida bills the federal government $85 per hour for skilled nursing visits. The registered nurse performing that visit earns $22 per hour. The home health aide performing personal care visits is billed at $42 per hour; she earns $13. The difference — the spread between what Medicare pays and what the worker receives — flows through a layered ownership structure: the operating company, a management company collecting an 8% fee, and in many cases a REIT holding the office facilities.

The gap is not wages minus profit. It is wages minus the entire extraction architecture: management fees, LLC layers, REIT rent, debt service on acquisition leverage, and administrative overhead generated by compliance requirements that do not improve care. The Medicare program is publicly funded. The extraction flows to private financial actors. The care workers and the patients bear the consequences of what is extracted.

The extraction architecture makes that continuity economically irrational.

The mean Adjusted Payment Percentage across all agencies was +0.30% — approximately $1,500/year. The extraction architecture produces a $29/hour gap per aide. The VBP incentive is structurally insufficient to change the wage by design: it operates at a different leverage level than the ownership structure that determines wages. This is the T2/T1 distinction made concrete and personal.

Case 2: Gig Economy Misclassification — Uber & Lyft in Florida

Uber and Lyft classify their drivers as independent contractors rather than employees. This classification is not a neutral description of the employment relationship — it is a structural choice that determines which Nussbaum capabilities workers can access. Independent contractor status removes the employer’s obligation to provide minimum wage guarantees, overtime pay, unemployment insurance, workers’ compensation, and the right to organize under the National Labor Relations Act.

The “flexibility” argument is the Triadic Hack applied to labor. Uber’s marketing tells drivers they are “entrepreneurs” with “freedom” and “flexibility.” The Sign is intact (drivers can choose their hours). The Signal is intact (the app accurately shows earnings per trip). The Interpretant has been replaced: “flexibility” has been substituted for “employment security,” and the driver experiences this substitution as a feature of their own autonomous choice rather than as a structural design that transfers risk from the corporation to the worker.

Case 3: Single-Family REIT Housing Extraction — Invitation Homes

Invitation Homes is the largest single-family rental REIT in the United States, owning approximately 80,000 homes across 16 markets. It was created through a post-2008 financial crisis strategy of bulk-purchasing foreclosed homes at distressed prices with private equity backing (Blackstone), then converting them to rental properties and taking the company public as a REIT in 2017. The REIT structure requires distributing 90% of taxable income as dividends, which creates a structural preference for revenue extraction over property maintenance investment.

The maintenance trap is structural, not individual. When a REIT must distribute 90% of taxable income, property renovation is disadvantaged: capital expenditure on renovation reduces current taxable income, lowering the dividend that the REIT structure requires. Deferred maintenance preserves the dividend. The $25M FTC fine for illegal fees represents approximately 4.5% of one year’s dividends — economically rational to absorb as a cost of doing business. This is the T2 regulatory response to a T1 structural failure, in real time, confirmed by the numbers.

What housing extraction does to community FHRI_max

The home is not just a financial asset. It is the physical substrate of the capability floor Nussbaum identifies as essential to human agency. When housing becomes a vehicle for extraction rather than a stable platform for family and community development, the FHRI_max of the community falls — not metaphorically but structurally:

💫
Residential instability suppresses long-horizon planning
Sapolsky’s research establishes that chronic housing insecurity produces glucocorticoid loads that suppress prefrontal cortex function — the biological substrate of future planning, delayed gratification, and complex ethical reasoning. Housing extraction is not an economic harm that exists separately from individual agency. It degrades the biological conditions for agency directly.
🏪

Eviction rates 5x higher than small landlords means higher student mobility rates in affected school districts. Research (Grigg 2012) shows each school move costs students approximately 0.36 standard deviations in academic achievement. The extraction architecture imposes educational costs on children who had no role in the transaction.
🌞

Physical entropy (φ) in our IPS framework measures substrate degradation. When 80,000 homes defer maintenance simultaneously, the physical quality of the surrounding neighborhood degrades. The 2% neighboring property value decline documented in the research is the external measurement of φ accumulation being externalized by the extraction architecture onto adjacent property owners who made no financial transaction with Invitation Homes.
The FTC fine demonstrates the T2 trap precisely. The regulatory response (T2: fine the operator for illegal fees) does not address the structural configuration (T1: the REIT dividend distribution requirement that makes maintenance economically irrational). After the fine, Invitation Homes continues operating with the same structural parameters. The fine is absorbed as a cost of the extraction architecture. Nothing structural has changed.

SAGE Applied: The Home Health Case

SAGE is a tool, not a ritual. The four steps — Situation, Affected, Grounds, Evaluate — are meant to prevent the most common ethical reasoning errors: stopping at the visible agent instead of the structural architecture, applying only one ethical tradition instead of four, and jumping to action without understanding what leverage level your action reaches.

Situation: What is structurally happening?

Before asking “what should be done,” SAGE requires accurate structural description. The most common error in economic ethics is naming the visible agent (the low-wage employer) when the causal structure extends to the ownership architecture, the regulatory framework, and the financial incentive system that determines the employer’s options.

The home health situation has four distinct structural layers, each with its own ethical claim:

LAYER 1 — FINANCIAL EXTRACTION (ε = 0.32–0.64 by ownership type)
Management fees, REIT rents, PE debt service, and LLC structural separation extract 29–69% of Medicare revenue before it reaches the worker. This is the structural cause. No amount of individual employer goodwill changes the arithmetic of this architecture.
LAYER 2 — REGULATORY DESIGN (ρ and μ)
The VBP program produces +$1,500/year average incentive. The worker classification framework permits contractor misclassification. The ownership disclosure requirements are insufficient to make ε visible to regulators or families. These are policy choices, not natural conditions.
LAYER 3 — LABOR MARKET STRUCTURE (monopsony)
In many Florida counties, 3–5 large home health chains control 70%+ of aide employment. This is a monopsony condition — workers face a limited number of buyers for their labor, suppressing wages below competitive market rates independent of any individual employer decision.
LAYER 4 — EXTERNALIZED COSTS
The extraction architecture externalizes its costs onto: the public Medicaid system (aide poverty drives Medicaid enrollment), informal family caregivers (predominantly women, $470B/year unpaid), the patients who receive less continuous care (higher hospitalization rates), and communities with degraded care workforce capacity.
The structural diagnosis: The situation is not “employers pay low wages.” The situation is “a financial architecture extracts 69% of public Medicare funding before it reaches the care worker, while a regulatory framework produces incentive adjustments of $1,500/year that cannot change this extraction rate.” These two descriptions lead to radically different ethical evaluations and different action strategies.

Affected: Who bears the consequences?

SAGE requires mapping the full web of impact — not just the immediately visible parties. In economic structural analysis, the most important affected parties are often those farthest from the visible transaction.

For Mrs. Chen specifically: her independence, dignity, and safety depend on a care relationship that the extraction architecture makes economically irrational to sustain.

Immediate harm: public funds flow through the extraction architecture and reach the care worker at 31 cents on the dollar. Systemic harm: the extraction architecture is publicly subsidized. Structural harm: communities where care work is poorly compensated develop workforce shortages (people leave the sector). The shortage worsens the extraction opportunity (scarcity drives demand). This is the self-reinforcing loop of the care economy: extraction produces shortage, shortage justifies billing rates, billing rates fund further extraction, shortage persists. The community’s capacity to care for its aging population is structurally consumed.

Grounds: What the ethical traditions say

Different ethical traditions illuminate different aspects of the same structural situation. No single tradition is sufficient. The IPS framework connects them by identifying what each tradition is tracking in the structural configuration.

Care Ethics (Gilligan, Noddings, Held)

Care Ethics begins with the web of relationships of dependency and asks: who is most vulnerable? What do those relationships of care require? The home health situation reveals a structural violation of care: Maria’s relationship to Mrs. Chen is one of genuine care — she knows her patient’s preferences, her fall risks, her family dynamics. This relational knowledge is the actual source of the safety that prevents hospitalization.

The extraction architecture treats this relational knowledge as having zero economic value. The billing rate reflects the hour, not the relationship. High turnover destroys the relationship and resets the care knowledge to zero — exactly the moment when the patient is most vulnerable. Care Ethics identifies this as not merely unjust but as a category error: the economic system has failed to recognize what care actually is.

Care Ethics verdict on the extraction architecture: It violates the fundamental obligation of institutions that engage in relationships of care dependency to maintain the conditions that make genuine care possible. FHRI_max = 14 in a care institution is an institutional commitment to providing the appearance of care while structurally preventing its substance.
Fairness & Justice (Rawls, Nozick, Walzer)

Rawls behind the veil of ignorance: Would you design the home health labor architecture if you didn’t know whether you’d be the PE investor, the agency operator, the aide, Mrs. Chen, or Mrs. Chen’s daughter? The answer is unambiguously no. The veil of ignorance test fails: no one would rationally choose a system that extracts 69% of public care funding before it reaches the worker, if they faced equal probability of being that worker.

Nozick’s entitlement theory: Even on libertarian grounds, the extraction architecture has a problem. Medicare reimbursement is a public contract for the delivery of care. The public pays for care services. The extraction architecture diverts public funds from care services to financial extraction. This is not a free market transaction — it is a contractual violation of the terms under which the public funds were allocated.

Walzer on spheres of justice: The care economy is a sphere in which money should not be the dominant good. Transforming care relationships into financial extraction vehicles violates the principle that different spheres of social life have their own appropriate distributive logic. The home health extraction architecture colonizes the sphere of care with the logic of financial extraction.
Consequentialism (Mill, Singer, Effective Altruism)

Consequentialism is strongest here because we now have quantified outcomes. The comparison is not between one ethical system and another — it is between two ownership structures with measured outcome differences:

Non-profit home health agencies (FHRI_max = 62.9): DTC rate 74.9%, lower hospitalization rates, lower turnover (22% vs 35%), better care continuity scores.

Proprietary agencies (FHRI_max = 37.3): DTC rate 72.1%, higher hospitalization rates, higher turnover, lower care continuity.

The 25.6-point FHRI_max gap produces measurable patient outcome differences. The aggregate consequence of the extraction architecture, applied across 12,239 home health agencies and 8 million patients annually, is quantifiable in hospitalizations prevented, falls avoided, and quality-adjusted life years maintained or lost.

Consequentialist verdict: The extraction architecture produces worse aggregate outcomes than the structural alternative (non-profit ownership) at every measured point. Maximizing aggregate welfare requires T1 structural reform of ownership architecture, not T2 optimization within the existing extraction structure.
Virtue Ethics & Duty (Aristotle, Kant, MacIntyre)

Aristotle on the telos of institutions: Every institution has a proper function (telos) — the purpose that defines what excellence (arete) looks like for it. A home health agency’s telos is the provision of quality care enabling patient independence and dignity. An agency structurally configured to maximize financial extraction at the expense of care quality has corrupted its own telos. MacIntyre would say it has substituted “external goods” (financial return) for the “internal goods” (excellence in care) that define the practice.

Kant’s categorical imperative: Could we universalize the extraction architecture? If every institution of care were configured as PE-acquired nursing homes are, with FHRI_max = 14, the practice of care would cease to exist as a meaningful category. Universalization fails: the extraction architecture is parasitic on the existence of genuine care institutions that it is systematically destroying.

Duty of Maria: Maria has a genuine professional duty to her patients. The extraction architecture is not a reason to abandon that duty — it is a reason to recognize that the duty extends beyond individual care to include the obligation to name the structural condition accurately and refuse to normalize it. Filing incident reports, supporting union organizing, speaking to regulatory agencies: these are duty-fulfilling acts, not supererogatory heroism.
Capabilities Approach (Nussbaum, Sen)

Nussbaum asks not “how much do people earn?” but “what are people actually able to DO and BE?” The capability framework maps directly onto FHRI: the Functional Health Resilience Index measures how much of an agent’s capacity to coordinate coherent action is structurally available in their institutional context.

Maria earns $13/hour. Below the capability threshold for:
• Bodily health: no employer health insurance, high injury rates in top-5 most-injured occupation
• Practical reason: below Sapolsky threshold for planning beyond 2 weeks
• Control over environment: no union rights as misclassified contractor in many agency structures
• Affiliation: 35% annual turnover destroys the ongoing relationships that constitute meaningful work

The crucial Nussbaum insight: Maria has the “formal freedom” to choose her employer. She lacks the “substantive freedom” to choose a structurally different work relationship, because the extraction architecture defines the terms of employment across most of the sector. Formal freedom inside structural extraction is not freedom. It is the appearance of freedom with the substance removed — the Triadic Hack applied to labor markets.

The evaluation must be honest about this — prescribing T1 interventions to actors who only have T2 access is ethically irresponsible, not aspirationally inspiring.

Cultural Resonance: Two Prior Art Texts

Science fiction does not illustrate the economics chapter. It discovered the structural dynamics before the data existed to prove them. These two texts are “prior art” — genuine intellectual precursors to the IPS framework that make the abstract parameters emotionally accessible. They occupy one tab, proportionate to their role: the entry point, not the primary content.

Wall-E as prior art for ε at civilizational scale

Buy-N-Large’s economic logic is identical to the PE-acquired nursing home’s: extract maximum resource flow, defer the costs onto the substrate (the Earth, the bodies of care workers, the communities dependent on care), maintain operational continuity while replacing the goal. The film’s achievement is compressing a 50-year extraction arc into 90 minutes with a robot as its conscience.

Wall-E’s economics
Buy-N-Large extracts all productive value from Earth’s ecosystem. ε approaches 1.0. φ (physical entropy) collapses the biological substrate. The Axiom passengers are the care economy’s patients: maintained in comfort (FHRI high), incapable of self-direction (FHRI_max near zero).
The Maathai principle in the ruins
Wall-E’s function — care, attention, memory — is the low-ε alternative. He does not fight Buy-N-Large. He tends the plant. The Cleveland Evergreen Cooperatives tend the plant. The community land trust tends the plant. The non-profit home health agency with FHRI_max = 62 tends the plant.
The IPS parameters of Buy-N-Large civilization: ε = 0.97 (near-total extraction), φ = 0.95 (Earth substrate destroyed), μ = 0.85 (corporate optimization aligned with shareholder return, not human flourishing), σ = 0.70 (700 years of accumulated operational procedure), ρ = 0.20 (minimal regulatory burden — regulation itself has been captured). FHRI_max for Axiom passengers: approximately 2. This is where the home health extraction trajectory leads if unaddressed.

Sorry to Bother You as prior art for μ as career trap

Cassius Green’s “white voice” is the career-level version of the same structural dynamic operating in home health. He learns that optimizing for the institutional metric (the performance Regalview rewards) produces institutional success and moral injury simultaneously. The information architecture passes the white voice. It suppresses the genuine voice. This is the information filter the VBP system runs: it passes compliance metrics; it suppresses the signal that care quality is collapsing under extraction pressure.

The WorryFree endpoint. The film’s satirical leap — a corporation that offers lifetime housing and food in exchange for labor servitude, then biologically modifies workers to optimize their productive capacity — is not as distant from the home health case as it appears. The extraction architecture already modifies workers: chronic low wages produce the specific stress physiology Sapolsky documents, suppressing prefrontal cortex function and locking workers in short-horizon survival cognition. The biological modification is already happening. WorryFree makes it explicit.
The strike sequence is the T1/T2 choice made visible. Cassius’s individual work performance (T2): excellent agent behavior within unchanged extraction parameters. The RegalView workers’ collective action (T1 attempt): organizing to change the structural conditions of extraction. The film is honest that T1 is harder and that individual agents face real costs for pursuing it. This is not cynicism. It is accurate structural analysis that the course validates with data.
