Research foundation: Peterson et al. and the CMS public record. The healthcare simulations in this module draw on the empirical research of Dr. Lindsay Peterson (Associate Professor of Research, University of South Florida School of Aging Studies) and her colleagues at the Florida Policy Exchange Center on Aging. Their 2025 AARP Florida report — Change of Ownership and Quality in Florida Nursing Homes — documented how private equity acquisition of 156 Florida facilities between 2019 and 2024 produced a 13% decline in direct care staffing, a doubling of one-star quality ratings, and $1,000 higher Medicare costs per resident. The IPS parameter values used here are derived directly from these published findings and from publicly accessible federal databases maintained by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS): the Payroll-Based Journaling staffing dataset, the Provider Information file, the Ownership disclosure file, and the Health Deficiencies citation database.
Why this matters beyond Florida. Florida's nursing home sector is a leading indicator, not an isolated case. Private equity acquisition of long-term care facilities is accelerating nationally — approximately 5% of all U.S. nursing homes are PE-owned, with REIT ownership adding another 9%. The structural dynamics documented in Florida — extraction-driven staffing reduction, billing-metric incentive restructuring, and quality rating decline — are visible in the federal data wherever ownership has transferred to investment vehicles that prioritize short-term surplus extraction over care coordination. What the simulation adds to Peterson's observational research is a causal mechanism: the five IPS parameters show why the outcomes differed across ownership types, not merely that they differed. This is SAGE in action — Seeing what the data documents, Analyzing the structural mechanism, Generating an intervention framework, Engaging with the ethical question of what ownership structures society should permit in settings that govern the final years of vulnerable lives.

Demo 1 — Four Florida Ownership Archetypes

Each panel shows the five IPS structural parameters and the computed ceiling values for that ownership type. All four facility types operate under identical state and federal regulatory burden (ρ = 0.700 — the same CMS documentation and MDS compliance requirements apply regardless of ownership). The differences in FHRI_max are produced entirely by ownership-structure differences in ε, φ, σ, and μ.

Demo 2 — PE Acquisition Trajectory: 2019 → 2024

The year-by-year IPS evolution of a facility that was acquired by private equity in 2020. The pre-acquisition baseline (2019) reflects a moderate for-profit profile. The acquisition year (2020) shows the ε jump and μ restructuring that occur when ownership transitions to investment-vehicle management. Subsequent years show φ accumulation (infrastructure underinvestment) and σ ratcheting (deficiency-driven procedural layering) as the structural consequences of sustained high extraction compound over time.

Demo 3 — Boisjoly Scenario (T4: High FHRI, High Parasitism)

Agent FHRI = 82 (highly competent, committed, high-ATCF professionals). IPS structural parameters reflect the NASA / Morton Thiokol institutional architecture circa January 1986. The FHRI_max shows how much of that agent capacity the structural architecture absorbs before it reaches decisions.

Demo 4 — Climate Governance Field: Four-Institution Structural Profiles

IPS profiles for the four institutions in the Ch14 climate governance multi-institutional field simulation. The structural gap between the Scientific Body and National Executive Governance explains why trust vector trajectories decline even as scientific consensus strengthens.
