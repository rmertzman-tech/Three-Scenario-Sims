SAGE and Its Limit

SAGE is the right instrument for most ethical analysis. Situation, Affected, Grounds, Evaluate — these four steps prevent the most common ethical reasoning errors and work for individual decisions, organizational dilemmas, and policy questions at any level of complexity. Use SAGE. It works.

SAGE has one structural limitation: its Situation step describes what is visible. In cases of institutional parasitism, what is visible is always the Sign — the operational continuity, the institutional function, the care that nominally occurs, the regulatory program that nominally measures. The Sign is always intact in a successful parasitic overwrite. That is the definition of successful overwrite.

This means SAGE, applied without Perception, will identify the administrator implementing the extraction as the structural agent, rather than the ownership architecture that determined the administrator’s options. It will identify the low-wage employer as the structural cause, rather than the financial architecture extracting 69% of Medicare revenue before wages are set. It will identify the regulatory program as an adequate response, rather than recognizing it as a T2 intervention structurally insufficient to address the T1 condition it nominally targets.

The specific failure: Without Perception, SAGE performs structural analysis on the Sign level, not the Interpretant level. It asks “what is happening?” (Sign) and “who is responsible?” (visible actor) rather than “which goal-function is now installed?” (replaced Interpretant) and “who achieved access to the channel?” (P-adic proximity mechanism). The difference is not minor. The Situation description changes completely, and so does the Evaluate step: you cannot design effective interventions from a Situation description that identifies the wrong causal level.

The Perception Step: Three Questions

Before asking what is happening (SAGE S), ask what has been hidden. The Perception step is the formal instrument for reading past the Sign to the Interpretant. Three questions, asked in sequence, identify the structural architecture before SAGE begins.
P1
Is the Sign intact?
Does the institution still appear to function? Does the operation nominally continue?

The Sign is the raw observable fact of institutional operation. In a nursing home: care visits occur, staff are present, the facility is open and licensed. In a home health agency: aides go to patient homes, documentation is filed, Medicare billing occurs. In a regulatory program: enforcement happens, citations are issued, fines are collected.

The critical insight: Sign preservation is the defining feature of successful parasitic overwrite, not evidence against it. If you answer P1 with “yes, the institution still functions,” this does not mean there is no parasitic overwrite. It means the overwrite has been successful enough that the operational camouflage is intact.

If the Sign is NOT intact — if the institution is visibly failing, openly dysfunctional, or has collapsed entirely — you are looking at a post-extraction condition, not an active parasitic overwrite. The ethical analysis then addresses causes of collapse, not the currently operating parasite. Most of the ethical situations the course addresses involve institutions where the Sign is intact: they still function, they still appear to serve their purpose, and the parasitic overwrite is invisible precisely because the Sign has been preserved.
P2
Has the Signal been manipulated?
What is the actual channel connecting Sign to Interpretant? Who has accessed it?

The Signal is the actual channel event that carries the Sign to the Interpretant. It is not the Sign itself — it is the encoding of the Sign into a form that produces an Interpretant (a meaning, a goal, a response). Identifying the Signal requires asking: what is the actual mechanism through which the institutional Sign (operations occurring) is translated into organizational decisions and responses?

Nursing home: The Signal is the budget allocation decision — how Medicare and Medicaid revenue is distributed across management fees, debt service, REIT rent, staffing, and care supplies. This is the channel through which the Sign (care operations occur) is translated into the Interpretant (what those operations are for and who they serve).
HHVBP regulatory program: The Signal is the payment adjustment (+0.30% mean APP). This is the channel through which the Sign (quality measurement occurring) is translated into the Interpretant (what the measurement means for structural behavior).
Challenger launch decision: The Signal is the management review process routing Boisjoly’s incident reports. This is the channel through which the Sign (engineering data on O-ring failure) is translated into the Interpretant (what that data means for the launch recommendation).

The P-adic access question: who has achieved P-adic proximity to this channel? Who is inside the hierarchical cluster controlling the Signal? In the nursing home: the ownership structure (PE fund, management company, REIT) is inside the budget cluster. In HHVBP: the regulatory design process is inside the payment architecture cluster. In Challenger: management authority is inside the review process cluster while engineering authority has been structurally excluded.

P-adic proximity is achieved through different routes at different organizational levels: biochemical internalization (molecular), ownership or employment authority (institutional), regulatory design access (sector), political and lobbying access (governance), infrastructure control (civilizational). The route differs. The formal consequence is identical: complete access to the Signal channel for that hierarchical level.
P3
Has the Interpretant been replaced?
What goal-function does the Signal now serve? What was the original?

The Interpretant is the meaning-assignment function: what the Signal means for action, what goal the institutional architecture is now optimizing for. Identifying Interpretant replacement requires holding two questions simultaneously: what goal does this institution nominally exist to serve? (the original Interpretant) and what goal does the observable behavior of the institutional decision-making machinery actually optimize? (the current Interpretant).

Original Interpretant
The goal-function the institution was designed to serve.

Nursing home: provide quality care enabling resident dignity and wellbeing.
VBP: create incentives large enough to change quality-related structural behavior.
Challenger review: ensure launch decisions reflect all available engineering safety data.
Replaced Interpretant
The goal-function the decision-making machinery actually optimizes.

Nursing home: maximize management fee extraction subject to regulatory compliance constraints.
VBP: produce measurement outputs and payment adjustments within regulatory design constraints.
Challenger review: confirm the launch recommendation that schedule pressure makes desirable.

The measured gap between original and replaced Interpretant is μ (incentive misalignment). The mechanism sustaining the replacement is σ (morphic residue — accumulated normalization that makes the replaced Interpretant feel like the original). The magnitude of the replacement is ε (extraction rate — how much of the original goal-function has been displaced by extraction goals).

The Perception step is complete when you can state: (1) The original Interpretant was [X]. (2) The current Interpretant is [Y]. (3) The Signal channel through which the replacement was achieved is [Z]. (4) The actor that achieved P-adic proximity to channel Z is [W]. This four-element statement is the P-step output that grounds everything in SAGE that follows.

Three Teaching Depths

Depth 1: P-SAGE as a Vocabulary Extension (15 minutes)

At this depth, P-SAGE simply adds three questions to the beginning of SAGE. No formal derivation. No P-adic geometry. No Peircean semiotic theory. The questions are presented as practical checks that prevent the most common ethical analysis error: naming the visible agent when the structural cause is the architecture behind the agent.

Before you do SAGE, ask three questions:

1. Is the institution still functioning normally? (If yes, the problem may be hidden beneath the surface.)
2. What is the actual channel through which decisions are made — and who controls that channel?
3. What is the institution nominally supposed to be doing, and what is it actually optimizing for? Are those the same?

If the answers reveal a gap between what the institution appears to serve and what it actually serves, you have found the structural wrong before SAGE begins.

Teaching note: at this depth, the P step takes 5 minutes and is presented as a “pre-SAGE check.” Students learn to ask the channel-control question and the Interpretant-replacement question without knowing the formal names. The vocabulary (Interpretant, P-adic, Triadic Hack) is optional enrichment, not required for this depth.

When to use D1: First introduction to structural ethics. Students who are new to the IPS framework. Situations where class time is limited. Any context where the vocabulary burden of the full formal method would prevent engagement with the core insight.
Depth 2: P-SAGE as Diagnostic Protocol (45 minutes)

At this depth, P-SAGE is taught using the Semiotic Rosetta Stone as its primary tool. Students see the Sign-Signal-Interpretant structure at three or four levels (molecular, individual, institutional, sector) and learn to recognize the pattern: Sign intact + Signal accessed + Interpretant replaced. The formal derivation is not required, but the structural mechanism is made visible through multiple examples.

Teaching sequence for D2: (1) Screen the hairworm video (2 minutes). Ask students to identify Sign, Signal, Interpretant without those words yet. (2) Introduce the vocabulary by naming what students just identified. (3) Show the Rosetta Stone for the Challenger case. Ask students to find the corresponding elements. (4) Show the nursing home case. Ask students to apply the same three-question structure. (5) Apply P-SAGE to a new case students have not seen.

What D2 students can do:
Identify which SSI component has been corrupted in a new case. Recognize the Triadic Hack across different organizational levels. Apply the three Perception questions before SAGE. Identify the P-adic access mechanism at the institutional and sector levels.
What D2 omits:
The formal P-adic metric derivation. The Triadic Separability Theorem. The formal proof that the mechanism is a law rather than a pattern. The mathematical relationship between IPS parameters and semiotic components.
When to use D2: Students who have already used SAGE in multiple cases and are ready for the structural mechanism behind it. Policy and advocacy students preparing for regulatory or institutional reform work. Advanced EBB students in the final third of the course.
Depth 3: P-SAGE as Formal Method (Full session)

At this depth, P-SAGE is taught as a formal theorem with a derivation. The session establishes: (1) the P-adic metric structure of hierarchical organizations and the ultrametric inequality; (2) the Peircean triadic necessity and the architectural separability of SSI components; (3) the joint entailment of the scale-free parasitic overwrite law; (4) the formal identification of each IPS parameter as a measurement of a specific SSI component or access condition; and (5) the FHRI_max as residual Interpretant integrity.

This depth is appropriate for: researchers using the IPS framework to generate new empirical predictions; policy analysts designing T1 regulatory instruments; graduate students or advanced undergraduates in ethics, organizational theory, or computational social science; PBC founding board members who need to understand the formal basis of the measurement they are certifying.

In a hierarchical organization with P-adic metric structure, a parasitic actor achieving P-adic proximity to a cluster C_L (achieving membership at the cluster’s radius r) has access to the information channel I_L controlled by C_L equivalent to any existing member.

The Triadic Separability Theorem:

Joint entailment (the Scale-Free Law):

The mechanism holds at every level where hierarchical structure and information transmission are both present. Both conditions are present in every human-scale institutional system.
