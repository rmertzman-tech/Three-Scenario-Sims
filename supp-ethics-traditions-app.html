The Traditions Behind the Framework: Six Ethical Perspectives Through the EBB Lens

Where each tradition came from · What each one sees · What each one contributed to the ACP

How to Use This Chapter

This chapter can be placed anywhere in the EBB course after Chapter 5. You have enough vocabulary — PRF, suppression gap, λ Raw, λ Perceived, ATCF, the five worldview distributions — to engage fully with each tradition. Everything else is introduced here as needed.

Chapter 8 shows what each ethical tradition measures in the simulation. This chapter asks the prior question: where did each tradition come from, and why does it see what it sees? The answer, in every case, is that each tradition was designed to solve a specific historical problem — a failure, injustice, or inadequacy in the moral thinking that preceded it.

The Four Lenses

Each tradition is analyzed through four consistent lenses:

1. The Historical Problem — what specific crisis or failure prompted this tradition?

2. The PRF It Holds — what does it make immediately visible? What does it structurally struggle to see?

3. The Simulation Lens — which metric best captures its distinctive discovery?

4. ACP Contribution — what did this tradition discover that the Agency Conditions Principle requires?

The Argument in One Paragraph

Each tradition solved a real problem by developing a distinctive way of seeing — and in doing so, developed a structural blind spot that the next tradition would identify. The six traditions form a cumulative genealogy: Virtue Ethics → Kant → Mill → Care Ethics → Rawls → Nussbaum. The Agency Conditions Principle is not a seventh tradition that supersedes the others. It is the structure that holds all six partial perceptions simultaneously. Navigate the tabs in sequence the first time through, then return to any tradition when it becomes relevant to a case study or applied sequence chapter.

Tradition One

Virtue Ethics

Aristotle · ca. 350 BCE · Later: MacIntyre, contemporary revival

The Historical Problem

Aristotle wrote the Nicomachean Ethics in the aftermath of Macedonian conquest and Athenian political instability. The question was existential: if the polis — the city-state that is the natural context for human flourishing — is fragile and often corrupt, what makes a human life go well? What kind of person can live well when external political stability cannot be guaranteed?

The moral traditions that preceded Aristotle were either too dependent on divine command (telling you what to do but not what kind of person to become) or too focused on abstract principles (applicable universally but giving little guidance for particular situations). Aristotle's innovation was to ask differently: instead of "what is the right action?" he asked "what is the right kind of person to be?" The answer: someone who has developed stable character dispositions through practice and habituation, so that excellent action becomes the natural expression of who they are rather than effortful compliance with a rule.

The PRF Virtue Ethics Holds

✓ Makes immediately visible
Character development over time — the trajectory of who someone is becoming, not just what they do in any given moment
Habituation as the mechanism of moral development — how repeated action crystallizes into stable disposition
Eudaimonia (flourishing) as the measure of a life — neither compliance with rules nor maximization of outcomes but genuine human thriving
The moral significance of emotion — the virtuous person feels the right way, with neither excess nor deficiency
Phronesis (practical wisdom) — the capacity to perceive what a situation requires, irreducible to rule-following
✗ Structurally struggles to see
Those without access to conditions for virtue cultivation — in Aristotle's own account: the enslaved, women, non-citizens
Structural injustice that makes virtue cultivation impossible regardless of individual effort — you cannot develop practical wisdom in permanent survival mode
Obligations to strangers, future generations, or distant populations we will never meet
Situations requiring rapid institutional design decisions rather than slow character formation over a lifetime

Tradition Two

Deontological Ethics

Immanuel Kant · 1785 · Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals

The Historical Problem

Kant wrote the Groundwork in 1785, during the European Enlightenment, targeting a specific philosophical crisis: the arbitrariness of moral authority. For centuries, moral obligation had been grounded in divine command, natural law interpreted by the Church, or royal decree. All three have the same structural problem: morality depends on who happens to have power.

Kant's problem was to find a foundation for moral obligation independent of all external authority — one a rational agent could discover through reason alone. His answer: act only according to the maxim you can at the same time will to be a universal law. The second formulation goes further: treat persons always as ends, never merely as means. This was a direct response to the systematic instrumentalization of persons — slavery, serfdom, child labor — by institutions claiming moral legitimacy from tradition or authority.

The PRF Deontological Ethics Holds

✓ Makes immediately visible
The dignity of persons as non-negotiable — things you cannot do to people regardless of consequences
The corruption of instrumentalization — using persons as means to ends rather than as ends in themselves
Universal obligation — moral requirements applying to all rational agents regardless of culture or position
The moral weight of will and intention — what makes an action worthy is acting from duty, not from consequence
Institutional integrity — promise-keeping and rule-following have value independent of consequences
✗ Structurally struggles to see
Agents whose capacity for rational agency is compromised by material conditions — survival mode undermines the autonomous rational will Kant assumes
Situations where rigid universal rules produce catastrophic results for specific populations
The thickness of actual human lives beneath the abstraction of "rational agent"
Structural injustice that makes the exercise of formal rights impossible without specifying what must be done to enable them

Simulation Lens: Morphic Suppression · Suppression Gap

⚡ Generic institutional suppression configuration — illustrates Kant's non-instrumentalization claim across any whistleblower-type scenario

The Kantian question: are agents being treated as ends or as means? The suppression gap measures what Kant called instrumentalization — the distance between what an agent can actually contribute (λ Raw) and what the institutional architecture allows to reach decisions (λ Perceived).

When the Challenger engineers' knowledge failed to reach the launch decision, the teleconference architecture was, in Kantian terms, instrumentalizing them — using their presence to satisfy a procedural requirement while routing their actual signal around the decision. Morphic suppression measures the accumulated learned self-censorship that follows: agents whose contributions are consistently ignored begin to internalize the institutional judgment that their signal does not matter.

Deontological ethics discovered that coherent agency has a dignity condition — the non-negotiable protection of each agent's signal from being instrumentalized by institutional architecture. The suppression gap is not merely a coordination failure. It is a moral violation: agents whose knowledge and judgment are systematically excluded are being treated as means. The ACP's requirement that genuine capacity reach decisions is a Kantian requirement applied through simulation vocabulary.

Tradition Three

Consequentialism

Jeremy Bentham · 1789 · Refined by John Stuart Mill · 1863

The Historical Problem

Bentham published in 1789 — the year of the French Revolution — as an explicit reform tool targeting English common law and the aristocratic privilege embedded in it. The hedonic calculus — the greatest happiness of the greatest number — was designed as a reform criterion that could override tradition, class interest, and inherited privilege. If a law produces more suffering than happiness, change it, regardless of how long it has existed or whose interests it protects.

Mill refined Bentham in two important ways: pleasures differ in quality, not just quantity; and more crucially for the EBB framework, the utilitarian calculus makes the welfare of distant, invisible, and politically powerless populations morally relevant for the first time. The factory worker's suffering counts. The colonial subject's welfare counts. Future generations' interests count. This egalitarian reach — that all affected populations' claims must enter the moral calculus — is consequentialism's most enduring contribution.

The PRF Consequentialism Holds

✓ Makes immediately visible
Aggregate welfare — the total effect on all affected populations, weighted equally regardless of social status
Measurable outcomes — empirical evidence about what actually happens rather than what theoretically should
The claims of distant and invisible populations — future generations, faraway communities, the politically voiceless
The reformist critique of tradition — existing arrangements must justify themselves by what they produce
Proportionality — moral weight tracks scale of effects, not identity of affected parties
✗ Structurally struggles to see
Distribution within the aggregate — a policy can maximize total welfare while systematically disadvantaging specific populations
Rights of minorities against welfare-maximizing majorities — utility maximization is compatible with serious rights violations
The incomparability of different kinds of goods — some things cannot be traded off against others
Long-run costs that the calculus systematically discounts — the temporal commons problem is partly a failure of consequentialist accounting

Simulation Lens: OS2 Bridge · λ Perceived Mean

⚡ Generic resource-scarcity configuration — illustrates Mill's aggregate welfare claim across a heterogeneous multi-worldview population

The consequentialist question: does the coordination architecture produce the best outcomes for the largest number? OS2 Bridge measures cross-worldview coordination capacity — whether agents from different PRF positions can reach productive exchange. A high OS2 Bridge in Configuration B versus Configuration A is the simulation's consequentialist indicator.

Note what the consequentialist metric cannot see: the distribution of λ Perceived across agents. The mean may be higher in Configuration B while some agents remain far below it. That distributional question requires Rawls. Run the simulation and ask: what is the mean? Then ask: what is the range? The first question is Mill's. The second is Rawls'.

Contribution to the Agency Conditions Principle

Consequentialism discovered that the conditions for coherent agency must include the claims of all affected populations — not only those with political voice or institutional standing. The ACP's insistence that coherence-preserving governance accounts for populations rendered invisible by modeling assumptions (the Farmer critique of Chapter 7) is a consequentialist requirement: all populations' agency conditions count, and architectures that average them away are morally deficient because they produce worse outcomes for real people who were never in the model.

Tradition Four

Care Ethics

Carol Gilligan · 1982 · Nel Noddings, Joan Tronto · subsequent development

The Historical Problem

Carol Gilligan published In a Different Voice in 1982 as a direct critique of Lawrence Kohlberg's theory of moral development. Kohlberg's six-stage developmental sequence was derived entirely from male subjects and treated contextual, relational, care-based reasoning as a lower developmental stage. Women consistently scored at the conventional stage — not because they were morally underdeveloped, Gilligan argued, but because the scale was calibrated not to hear the moral voice they were developing.

The deeper historical problem runs further back than Kohlberg. Liberal moral theory — from Locke through Rawls — constructs its moral agent as an autonomous, rational, self-sufficient adult who enters social arrangements by choice. This is a fiction, and it is a gendered fiction. Every such autonomous agent was once a helpless infant, will probably be a vulnerable elder, and may at various points be ill, disabled, or dependent. The caregiving labor that makes autonomous adult life possible is systematically invisible in liberal theory — performed predominantly by women, uncompensated, treated as private rather than political, and excluded from the moral calculus that governs public life.

The PRF Care Ethics Holds

✓ Makes immediately visible
Dependency and vulnerability as the baseline of human existence, not exceptions to be managed
Relational obligation — the moral weight of particular relationships and the specific duties they generate
The invisible labor that sustains social life — caregiving, domestic work, emotional labor, the infrastructure of human connection
Context and particularity — what is required for this specific person in this specific situation
The moral significance of emotion — care and empathy as morally relevant, not as biases to be overcome
✗ Structurally struggles to see
How to scale care beyond personal relationships — works for face-to-face obligations, struggles with institutional design or obligations to strangers
The risk of reinforcing traditional gender roles by valorizing care work without addressing its gendered distribution
Obligations to future generations, distant communities, or people we will never meet
How to adjudicate between competing care relationships when resources are limited

Contribution to the Agency Conditions Principle

Care ethics discovered that coherent agency requires relational infrastructure — that the community block field is not a peripheral variable but a load-bearing structural condition. An agent's capacity to exercise genuine agency depends on whether they are embedded in networks of relationship, support, and mutual recognition. Institutions that claim to serve persons while systematically severing the relational infrastructure that makes their agency possible are failing the care ethics test regardless of their formal commitments.

Tradition Five — In Dialogue

Modern Justice Theories

John Rawls (1971) and Martha Nussbaum (1990s–2006) · Procedure meets Substance

The Shared Historical Problem

Both Rawls and Nussbaum are responding to the same failure: the inadequacy of utilitarian reasoning for institutional design. Mill showed that all populations' welfare counts and that reform requires empirical accountability. But the consequentialist framework aggregates in ways that make distributions invisible. You can maximize total welfare while systematically disadvantaging specific populations. You can average away the worst-off while reporting improvement in the mean.

Post-WWII liberal democracies needed to justify welfare state institutions without either socialist collectivism or libertarian minimalism. The utilitarian argument — welfare states produce better aggregate outcomes — was empirically vulnerable and failed to ground the intuition that something specific is owed to the worst-off that cannot be traded against aggregate gains for the majority.

Rawls: The PRF of Procedural Fairness

Rawls' central innovation: the original position — what principles would rational agents choose if they did not know their place in society, their class, their natural abilities, or any contingent fact about their identity? Strip away arbitrary advantages; ask what rational agents would endorse if they could not benefit from bias.

Two principles emerge: equal basic liberties for all; and the difference principle — inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society. This is the political philosophy complement to the ACP's temporal commons test: it asks whether the architecture improves conditions for those with the least, not just the average or majority.

✓ Rawls sees
The separateness of persons — the argument against aggregating welfare in ways that sacrifice individuals for the aggregate
The arbitrariness of natural talent and social position — advantages in competition are not morally deserved
The claims of the least advantaged — the difference principle as structural protection for those any other arrangement would leave behind
Procedural justice — fair procedures are independently morally required, not only fair outcomes
✗ Rawls struggles to see
Agents whose capacity to exercise formal primary goods is compromised by conditions the difference principle doesn't specify
Cultural variation in what primary goods mean across different populations
The threshold below which no one should be allowed to fall — the difference principle says improve the worst-off but not how much
The claims of persons with severe disabilities whose needs can't be captured in primary goods distributions

Nussbaum: The PRF of Substantive Capability

Nussbaum's foundational argument: the right question is not "what does this person have?" but "what can this person do and be?" Two people may have equal formal access to education while one — because of chronic illness, caregiving responsibilities, or economic precarity — is functionally incapable of using that access. Formal equality of primary goods cannot guarantee substantive equality of capability.

Nussbaum proposes ten central human capabilities — including bodily health, practical reason, affiliation, and control over one's political and material environment — each of which must be secured above a minimum threshold. Justice requires not equal capabilities but that no person fall below the threshold for any of them.

✓ Nussbaum sees
What people can actually do and become, not just what they formally have access to
The ten central capabilities as threshold conditions that institutional design must guarantee
The feminist argument that Rawls' primary goods framework undervalues care work and dependency
The development economics critique: GDP and income metrics mask real deprivation
✗ Nussbaum struggles to see
Who defines the list of capabilities and from whose standpoint
Cultural variation in how capabilities are expressed and prioritized
The tension between capability thresholds and individual liberty — ensuring capability sometimes requires constraining choices
How to implement specific threshold requirements consistently across diverse societies

Simulation Lens: Distributional Spread of λ Perceived

⚡ Generic coherence-preserving configuration — illustrates Rawls' difference principle and Nussbaum's capability threshold across a diverse agent population

This tradition asks a different question than the others: not the primary metric but the distribution across agents. The Rawlsian question: in which configuration does the lowest-λ Raw agent perform best? The difference principle says the architecture is more just if it makes the worst-off better off — not just the average.

The Nussbaumian question adds a threshold: is there a minimum λ Perceived below which no agent should fall, regardless of what the architecture produces on average? These are related but distinct. An architecture could satisfy the difference principle while still leaving some agents below a meaningful capability threshold.

Contribution to the Agency Conditions Principle

Rawls contributed the structural argument that coherence-preserving governance must evaluate itself by what it produces for the least advantaged — the difference principle applied to λ Perceived distributions. Nussbaum contributed the substantive specification: the nine fairness dimensions of Chapter 11 are Nussbaumian in structure, specifying threshold conditions rather than only procedural fairness. Together they ground the ACP's temporal commons test: not only must conditions improve for the least advantaged now, but the architecture must preserve and extend those improvements across generations.

Synthesis: Six Partial Perceptions of the ACP

Each tradition was designed to solve a specific historical problem. Each developed a distinctive way of seeing. Each, in solving its problem, developed a blind spot — which became the historical problem that prompted the next tradition. Together they form a cumulative genealogy of moral insight.

Virtue Ethics discovered that agency is developmental — that the conditions for coherent ethical life must be cultivated over time. ACP link: Historical continuity component of ATCF; the temporal commons question applied to character formation.
Deontological Ethics discovered that agency has a dignity condition — persons cannot be instrumentalized by institutional architecture regardless of efficiency gains. ACP link: Suppression gap requirement; non-negotiability of morphic fidelity.
Consequentialism discovered that the claims of all affected populations must count — institutional design must answer for what it produces for everyone, including those invisible to the model. ACP link: OS2 Bridge requirement; the Farmer critique of representative-agent modeling.
Care Ethics discovered that relational infrastructure is a precondition for agency, not a peripheral feature. ACP link: Community block field; the inclusion dimension of coherence-preserving governance.
Rawlsian Justice discovered that institutional design must be evaluated by what it produces for the least advantaged — distributions matter, not only means. ACP link: Distributional requirement; difference principle applied to λ Perceived.
Capabilities Approach discovered that justice requires substantive specification of what agency conditions must include — threshold conditions, not only procedural fairness. ACP link: The nine fairness dimensions; capability-ATCF mapping; temporal commons test across generations.

The ACP as Synthesis

No single tradition can be the ACP. Virtue ethics without Kant produces character-based reasoning with nothing to say about the dignity of those systemically denied access to virtue's conditions. Kant without Mill produces deontological rigor that cannot account for invisible populations. Mill without care ethics produces welfare calculations that render dependency labor invisible. Care ethics without Rawls cannot generate principles for institutional design. Rawls without Nussbaum cannot specify the substantive capability threshold below which no person should fall.

Together they describe what the ACP names as a single normative requirement: that institutional design must preserve — and where degraded, repair — the conditions under which coherent agency is possible for all the populations it governs, across time, across generations, and at every level of the civilizational stack.
